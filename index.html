★ How to read this guide

Your exam asks you to take a real crisis and analyse the communication. So this guide is built the way you'll be tested: one case at a time. For each one you'll get the same logical chain —

  1. 📰 What happened — the story, plainly told.
  2. 🧭 Read the crisis — why it's this type of crisis (the classification logic).
  3. 🗣️ What they said & did — the real statements, and the move behind each.

  5. ⚖️ Did it work? — the verdict.

Don't study the theory and hunt for examples. Do the reverse: understand the story, and the theory falls out of it. Each case "owns" one main framework; later cases reuse the earlier ones, so by the end you'll have seen everything two or three times in action.

Case 1

BP & the Deepwater Horizon oil spill

🧪 Teaches: SCCT — W. Timothy Coombs

Gulf of Mexico · 20 April 2010 · 11 dead · 4.9 million barrels spilled · the largest spill in US history.

📰 What happened

The Deepwater Horizon drilling rig — owned by Transocean, leased by BP — exploded and sank. Eleven workers died. For weeks oil gushed into the Gulf; by June 2010, 1,100 miles of shoreline in Louisiana, Mississippi and Florida were polluted. Under pressure from President Obama, BP set up a $20 billion compensation fund. By mid-2011 BP had lost roughly a quarter of its market value; the final bill (2016) reached about $61.6 billion. So BP faced two crises at once: an environmental catastrophe, and a communication catastrophe — and it's the second one the course cares about.

🧭 Read the crisis — why BP owns this

Before you judge the response, judge the situation, because the right response depends entirely on it. Coombs gives four questions:

  • Type? A preventable crisis — it grew from operational and human-judgment failures BP could have controlled, so the public attributes high responsibility. (Contrast: a hurricane wrecking the rig would be a victim crisis with almost no blame.)
  • Severity? Extreme — deaths, ecological ruin, fishermen's livelihoods destroyed.
  • History? BP had prior safety incidents — an intensifying factor.
  • Reputation? Already weak on safety and environment — a second intensifier.
🧭 The logic

High blame + bad history + bad reputation = the public demands accountability, not excuses. This is exactly the situation where the theory says you must use your most accommodative response (accept responsibility, fix it, compensate). Hold that thought — then watch BP do the opposite.

🗣️ What they said & did — and the move behind each

Facing a high-blame crisis, BP and CEO Tony Hayward reached for the low-blame playbook. Each quote below is a strategy:

"Well, it wasn't our accident… It was their rig, their equipment, their people."Hayward, blaming Transocean → shifting the blame / scapegoating
"The Gulf of Mexico is a very big ocean. The amount of oil… is tiny in relation to the total water volume." · "The environmental impact… is likely to be very, very modest."→ minimisation (the damage is small)
"A complex accident, caused by an unprecedented combination of failures… it is too early — and not up to us — to say who is at fault."→ a disclaimer that diffuses responsibility across "multiple parties"
"There's no one who wants this over more than I do. I'd like my life back."Hayward → makes himself the victim. Tone-deaf and self-centred.

And out of public view it was worse: BP tried to limit and delay the flow of information to the media, offered potential plaintiffs $5,000 to drop lawsuits, had no crisis plan, and even broke its own spill plan (which forbade promising the ecology would be "restored to normal"). Only later did it pivot to the right register: the $20bn fund, state-by-state cleanup websites, a research initiative, and TV ads where Hayward says "We will make this right."

🧪 The theory it teaches — SCCT (Coombs, 2007)

BP is the textbook case for Situational Crisis Communication Theory. Its whole logic rests on Attribution Theory: when something bad happens, people instinctively look for someone to blame. SCCT says your job is to read how much blame you'll get and match your response to it.

The 3 crisis types (rising blame)

Victim (you were hit too — disaster, rumour, tampering) → Accidental (a technical error) → Preventable (you should have stopped it — misdeed, negligence). Two intensifying factors push any crisis up the scale: a history of similar crises and a prior bad reputation.

The 3 response postures (rising accommodation)

Deny (attack the accuser · denial · scapegoat) → Diminish (excuse: we didn't mean it · justification: the harm is small) → Rebuild (compensation · apology). Plus bolstering as backup (remind people of past good works · praise stakeholders · note you're a victim too).

The rule

Low blame → you can deny/diminish. High blame → you must rebuild. And whatever you choose, stay consistent — a story that shifts reads as a lie.

Now BP's failure has a name: a preventable, high-blame crisis answered with deny + diminish — a textbook mismatch — delivered through a shifting story and a self-pitying spokesperson.

⚖️ Did it work?

No. The mismatch turned public anger into action: a "Boycott BP" Facebook group hit 640,000 members; a fake @BPGlobalPR account mocking BP gathered more followers (190,000) than BP's real account (18,000). BP lost ~25% of its value. The lesson landed in the negative: deny when you're guilty, and the audience writes your story for you.

💡 Why it matters (the transferable lesson)

When you own the crisis, denying or minimising doesn't reduce blame — it adds the charge of arrogance and dishonesty on top. The only response that works is to accept responsibility, fix the harm, compensate, and speak with one empathetic voice.

The mirror image (same lecture): in BP's 2025 shareholder revolt, the crisis was an accidental/management one (a strategy U-turn, no misconduct), so the right match was diminish + corrective action, no apology ("we went too far, too fast"; a leadership transition). Same company, different crisis type, opposite correct response — that contrast is SCCT.

🎯 In the exam

Classify it out loud (preventable · high severity · history · weak reputation → high blame). Name the wrong posture (deny/diminish) and call it a mismatch. Quote Hayward. Point to the broken consistency and the missing plan. Prescribe the fix (rebuild + one empathetic spokesperson). For a top mark, contrast with the 2025 revolt to prove you understand matching, not just labels.

Case 2

Carrefour & the boycott in China

🧪 Teaches: Apologia & Image Repair — Benoit

China · 2008 (Beijing Olympics year) · French hypermarket chain · accused of something it didn't do.

📰 What happened

2008 was a sensitive year: Beijing was hosting the Olympics (a huge prestige project), while human-rights groups abroad protested China's policy in Tibet and attacked the Games' sponsors. When the Olympic torch passed through Paris, protesters scuffled with a wheelchair-using Chinese athlete, Jin Jing, who was carrying the flame. Chinese citizens were furious. Then a rumour spread online that the French chain Carrefour was funding the Dalai Lama and supporting Tibetan independence. A boycott formed online, then spilled into the stores with real protests and damage. Carrefour had done nothing — it was caught by guilt by association (French company + anti-China feeling + a false rumour).

🧭 Read the crisis — a victim, but a fragile one

Here the classification flips BP on its head. Carrefour is the victim of a false accusation — low real responsibility. By SCCT, a victim crisis (especially a rumour) can be handled with denial. But the threat is to its image, and that's where a second, more precise toolkit takes over: Image Repair Theory asks not "how much are you to blame?" but "what exactly is the accusation, and who is the audience you must convince?"

🧭 The logic

Benoit says an attack has two parts: an offensive act + an accusation of responsibility. Carrefour faced the accusation ("you fund the Dalai Lama") without any real act. Remove the accusation and the threat disappears — so the whole job is to kill the rumour in the eyes of the Chinese public (the audience that matters).

🗣️ What they said & did

Carrefour's first move was a mistake the course flags: it stayed silent for a week while anger built. Then it acted well:

  • Denial — flatly rejected any link to pro-Tibet groups.
  • Adjusting information + sympathy — expressed concern for those affected.
  • Ingratiation (a "reducing offensiveness" move) — praised the Beijing Olympics and China.
  • A clean 3-step rumour-debunk: (1) acknowledge why people might believe it, (2) explain why it's untrue, (3) state the truth — its policy of staying out of politics and religion.
"We and all our employees feel regretful about what happened in Paris and support the Beijing Olympics 100 percent… Carrefour is innocent, but we understand the feeling of the Chinese people."Jean-Luc Lhuillier, VP, Carrefour China — denial + sympathy + ingratiation in one breath

The Chinese government, meanwhile, was conflicted: it didn't want the protests to overshadow the Olympics, so it discouraged the anti-French anger and urged citizens to express patriotism "rationally" — "the best way to love your country is to do your job well."

🧪 The theory it teaches — Apologia & Image Repair (Benoit, 1995)

This case introduces the family of image-defence theories. They start with Apologia — the rhetoric of self-defence (Greek apologia; note: not "apology"). Its four classic moves: denial, bolstering (point to your good deeds), differentiation (take the act out of its bad context) and transcendence (put it in a bigger, kinder context). Benoit systematised and expanded all this into Image Repair Theory — five categories you can mix and match:

  1. Denial — simple denial · shifting the blame
  2. Evasion of responsibility — provocation · defeasibility (no information/control) · accident · good intentions
  3. Reducing offensiveness — bolstering · minimisation · differentiation · transcendence · attack the accuser · compensation
  4. Corrective action — fix it / prevent recurrence
  5. Mortification — admit it and apologise

Carrefour's response now reads as a clean recipe: denial (cat. 1) + ingratiation and the rumour-debunk (cat. 3, reducing offensiveness). No corrective action or mortification was needed — because it was genuinely innocent.

⚠️ The honest limitation

Both apologia and IRT are descriptive, not evaluative — they name the moves but don't prove which one works; and IRT, used cynically, can help a guilty organisation dodge accountability. Worth a sentence in any essay about them.

⚖️ Did it work?

Largely, yes — once it stopped being silent. By denying clearly, showing cultural empathy, and praising the Olympics, Carrefour separated itself from the rumour and protected its brand in its most important market. The lasting lesson is in its one mistake: the week of silence.

💡 Why it matters

When you're innocent but accused, speed and audience-empathy are everything. Silence lets the rumour become the truth. Debunk it in three honest steps, show you understand why people were angry, and align yourself with what your audience holds dear (here: the Olympics, national pride).

🎯 In the exam

Frame it as a victim / "guilt by association" crisis. Name Benoit's categories used (denial + reducing offensiveness/ingratiation) and the 3-step rumour-debunk. Criticise the week of silence. Note the third actor — the government — managing the protests for its own Olympic interest.

Case 3

The Colectiv club fire

🧪 Teaches: public-sector crisis · consistency · framing (Iyengar)

Bucharest · 30 October 2015 · 27 → 65 dead, dozens injured · brought down a government.

📰 What happened

During a concert at the Colectiv club, indoor pyrotechnics set the foam ceiling alight. The initial death toll of 27 climbed to 65 as burn victims died over the following weeks — many because Romania's burn-care capacity was overwhelmed and patients had to be sent abroad. Public fury at corruption and lax safety enforcement exploded into mass protests; within days the Prime Minister, his government and the local mayor resigned. This is the course's deep dive into public-sector crisis communication (and it's the professor's own published research, so know it well).

🧭 Read the crisis

Apply Coombs' four factors: type = accident bordering on preventable (a foreseeable safety failure); severity = very high (health and life); history = no direct prior history; relationship = no specific prior negative one. The responsibility is shared and contested — between the club owners, the inspectors and the institutions — which is exactly why the communication became a fight over who is to blame.

🗣️ What they said & did — three failures to learn from

1) The club owners — shifting blame

They accepted only partial responsibility and pointed at the inspectors and authorities ("I have my share of responsibility, but I'd like it defined correctly… what institutions made mistakes"). In Benoit's terms: denial / shifting the blame + evading responsibility.

2) Raed Arafat (Secretary of State) — competent, but cold and defensive

The emergency chief gave detailed, technical explanations of patient transfers abroad and the international help received (Israel, France, UK, Norway, Finland). Accused of refusing foreign help, he used transcendence — burn-care capacity was limited across Europe, not just Romania, so this was bigger than him. But he showed little empathy, focused on medical detail, and attacked the accusers — the TV "experts" criticising the response ("the doctor talks without knowing how the systems work"). Defensive, not consoling.

3) The institutions — clashing voices

The good part: fast press releases from Arafat and the Health Minister. The fatal part: their statements contradicted each other. And the authorities largely ignored social media — the official Facebook page was used to post information (blood donations, victim locations) but never to interact (one post had 99 shares and a single reply).

🧪 The theory it teaches

Colectiv reuses SCCT and Benoit, but adds two ideas that matter for the exam:

① Consistency & one voice (the crisis-plan principle)

When Arafat and the Ministry contradicted each other, credibility leaked. This is the live demonstration of the plan's rule: one designated spokesperson, one consistent message. Contradiction in public = the audience stops trusting either source.

② Episodic vs thematic framing — Iyengar

How the media frames a crisis decides who the public blames. Episodic framing tells the story through individual events/victims → audiences blame individuals. Thematic framing sets it in context/trends → audiences blame the system. The civic movement "Corupția ucide" (Corruption Kills) used heavily thematic framing — pinning the fire on systemic corruption, not one club owner — which is exactly why it fed political consequences (resignations).

⚖️ Did it work?

As crisis communication, mostly no: empathy was thin, voices clashed, and the state ignored the conversation happening on social media. As a political event, the thematic "systemic corruption" frame won — the government fell, and President Iohannis took the unusual step of consulting civil society. The crisis exposed how far the public sector lagged the private one in communication.

💡 Why it matters

Two lessons stack here. First, being right isn't enough — Arafat was often factually correct but emotionally absent, and correctness without empathy or consistency still loses public trust. Second, whoever frames the story shapes the blame: master the difference between episodic and thematic framing, because it's how a single fire became a national reckoning.

🎯 In the exam

Use Colectiv to show range: classify it (Coombs), name the owners' shift-blame and Arafat's transcendence + attack-the-accuser (Benoit), diagnose the clashing voices + ignored social media (plan failures), and bring in Iyengar's episodic/thematic framing to explain the fallout. It's the case that lets you use four tools at once.

Case 4

Terror attacks — the first message

🧪 Teaches: the consolation script · "round-the-flag"

Nice 2016 (86†) · Brussels 2016 (32†) · London Bridge 2017 (7†) · Barcelona 2017 (13†).

📰 What happened

Four attacks across Europe, and four heads of government who had to stand up within hours and speak to a terrified nation. A terror attack isn't only deaths and damage — it deliberately manufactures panic, fear and division. So recovery depends on communication aimed at three audiences at once: the media, the general public, and the authorities' own internal staff. This case is about the very first message — and the remarkable thing is how similar all four leaders' messages were.

🧭 Read the crisis

An attack is the purest victim crisis: the nation is blameless, so there's no responsibility to deny or repair. That frees the communication to do the one thing it must — steady and unite the public while denying the attackers their real goal (fear and division). When blame isn't the question, emotion and meaning become the whole game.

🗣️ What they said — one shared script

Across all four cases the course finds the same five-part structure. This is the "first-message template":

  1. Express sympathy for the victims and their families.
  2. Give information on what happened + the measures being taken.
  3. Show appreciation for the emergency services and police.
  4. Emphasise unity & triumph over the threat — the "round-the-flag" effect.
  5. Invoke the values the attack targeted: democracy, liberty, human rights, unity.
"What we feared has happened… I call for calm and solidarity. We have to all face this unified as one."Charles Michel, Belgian PM, after Brussels — sympathy → unity
"The police responded with great courage and great speed… I want to thank and pay tribute to the professionalism and bravery of the police and the emergency services."Theresa May, after London Bridge — appreciation + round-the-flag

But within the shared script, the emphasis differed — and that's the analytical payoff:

  • Hollande (Nice) — heavy emotional language ("horror", "massacre"), and an outward turn to military action in Syria and Iraq.
  • May (London) — round-the-flag, praise for police, a four-point counter-terror plan, and the political line "enough is enough" (delivered during an election campaign).
  • Michel (Brussels) — calm, solidarity, unity above all.
  • Rajoy (Barcelona) — victims first, and a global framing (Madrid, Paris, Nice, Brussels, Berlin, London "have all felt the same pain").

🧪 The theory it teaches

This is the practical "first message" framework — you'll see it again in COVID. The deeper idea underneath is the "round-the-flag effect" (a threat to the group rallies people behind their leadership), plus the recognition that we live in a "risk society" primed for fear (Bakker, Furedi), which is what makes a calm, value-anchored first message so powerful.

⚖️ Did it work?

These are the course's positive models. The shared template works because it does exactly what an attack tries to prevent: it replaces panic with solidarity and reasserts the values under attack. The differences (emotion, the call to action, how each reads its country's history) are where leaders express their politics.

💡 Why it matters

In a blameless crisis, your job isn't defence — it's meaning and morale. Lead with the people, not the policy: sympathy first, then facts, then thanks, then unity, then the values that make "us" worth defending. It's the same instinct your own presentation argued the US skipped when it opened with victory and threat instead of consolation.

🎯 In the exam

If your case is an attack or disaster (a victim crisis), grade the leader's statement against the five elements, then say where they put the emphasis and why. Being able to draft such an opening, and to compare two leaders, is a likely question.

Case 5

COVID-19 — three countries, every theory

🧪 Teaches: risk perception & fear · EPPM (Witte)

UK · France · Romania · 2020–21 · the case where all the theories combine.

📰 What happened

A long, frightening, ongoing crisis where the entire job was communication: persuade millions of people to change their behaviour. The course compares how three governments did it — and uses them to teach how fear actually works.

🧭 Read the crisis — it's about perception, not just facts

One essential idea first: communicating risk is not the same as handing people data. People fear things out of proportion to the statistics — they over-fear the spectacular and uncertain, and under-fear the common. As Slovic put it, "people respond to the hazards they perceive."

💡 The example that explains it

People fear plane crashes far more than car crashes, though driving is far deadlier. Why? Flying feels uncontrollable, catastrophic, dreaded. Slovic's psychometric model says perceived risk rises with two factor-families: dread (uncontrollable, catastrophic, involuntary…) and the unknown (unobservable, delayed, new to science). Add Furedi's "culture of fear" (media amplify the worst case; trust in experts falls) and you see why a virus triggered such fear.

🧪 The theory it teaches — EPPM (Kim Witte)

If risk perception explains the fear, the Extended Parallel Process Model explains how to message it. Witte's rule is simple and powerful:

🧪 EPPM in one rule

A fear message works only if high threat is paired with high efficacy. Show people the danger is real (susceptibility + severity) and give them a doable action that works (response efficacy + self-efficacy). Then they do danger control — they act. Scare them with no way out, and they slide into fear control — denial, avoidance, switching off.

💡 Right vs wrong, in one line each

✅ "This virus spreads fast and can be serious — wash your hands for 20 seconds and wear a mask; it works." → people act.
❌ "The virus is everywhere and unstoppable." → no action offered → people panic or tune out.

🗣️ What they said & did — three styles

🇬🇧 United Kingdom — a tale of two voices

Boris Johnson was informal (sing "Happy Birthday" twice while washing hands), non-apologetic, and overestimated the country's readiness; analysts caught him using justification, transcendence and differentiation ("British exceptionalism"), plus EPPM's severity/susceptibility. Against him stood Queen Elizabeth II, whose 5 April 2020 address is the course's model: calm, rational, anchored in unity and "national spirit", and she pointedly never used the words "pandemic", "coronavirus" or "crisis". Same country, two registers — fear-management vs reassurance.

🇫🇷 France — accessibility as strategy

France leaned on making information reach everyone: a WhatsApp chatbot, the TousAntiCovid app, fact-checking services, easy-read FALC documents with pictograms, and flyers in 24 languages plus sign language. PM Castex ("Monsieur Déconfinement") was accessible but stumbled (blamed citizens; admitted not downloading the app). His speeches show the toolkit explicitly: transcendence ("all of Europe faces waves"), differentiation (France's results "more than honourable"), ingratiation (thanking citizens), and EPPM efficacy (our measures work, keep following them). Macron used warrior rhetoric — "victory", patriotism, a tone of national superiority.

🇷🇴 Romania — good intent, poor delivery

Decrees announced at 10–11 p.m., specialist jargon, and a PM (Cîțu) who rushed and mispronounced. The government had to fight a top-10 list of vaccine myths, and leaned more on warnings than on thanking compliant citizens. Arafat (yes — the Colectiv official) returned as a trusted-but-prickly information source.

⚖️ Did it work? & why it matters

The comparison is the lesson: the Queen succeeded by pairing calm reassurance with shared identity; France succeeded at reaching people; leaders failed when they scared without efficacy, blamed citizens, or delivered badly. COVID is the case where every theory shows up at once — Coombs (justification, ingratiation), Benoit (transcendence, differentiation), and above all Witte's EPPM.

🎯 In the exam

COVID is your "show everything" case. Explain risk perception (Slovic) and EPPM (Witte) first, then read a leader's choices through them. The Queen vs Johnson contrast is a ready-made compare-and-contrast answer; the "pair threat with efficacy" rule is the single most likely EPPM question.

Case 6

Qatargate — Eva Kaili's three interviews

🧪 Teaches: image repair tailored to each audience

European Parliament · scandal Dec 2022 · defence interviews June 2023 · France, Spain, Italy.

📰 What happened

Qatargate was a corruption scandal in the European Parliament; MEP Eva Kaili — previously a prominent, polished figure — was arrested over alleged ties to Qatar. After months in detention, in June 2023 she ran a deliberate communication counter-offensive: three newspaper interviews, in three countries, in three languages, within days — Libération (France), El Mundo (Spain) and Corriere della Sera (Italy). The fascinating part isn't that she defended herself — it's that she defended herself differently for each audience.

🧭 Read the crisis

This is a preventable-type crisis (alleged personal misconduct) with very high attributed blame and a hostile media. With guilt presumed, Kaili couldn't simply deny her way out — she had to reframe the story and rebuild credibility, audience by audience. So this is Image Repair Theory at its most sophisticated: same accusation, different audiences → different strategy mix.

🗣️ What she said — one scandal, three frames

Outlet (country)Central frameStrategies used
Libération (France)⚖️ Judicial injustice — mistreatment in detentiondenial · victimage · excuse/justification · transcendence
El Mundo (Spain)🕵️ Political conspiracy — Pegasus spying on MEPsdenial · justification ("gifts of equal value") · dissociation/defeasibility · transcendence · reminder
Corriere (Italy)❤️ Motherhood — her daughter used to pressure herequivocation · defeasibility · excuse/justification · transcendence
"These are souvenirs we exchanged for gifts of equal value… I can show you." (picks up a perfume, a statuette, promotional glasses)El Mundo — justification + reminder, recasting "bribes" as ordinary diplomacy

🧪 The theory it teaches — audience is everything

Remember Benoit's second question: who is the audience? Qatargate is the answer dramatised. The strategies are the same IRT menu you learned in Carrefour (denial, justification, transcendence, dissociation, victimage), but Kaili builds a layered pattern — legal in France, political in Spain, emotional in Italy — timing the releases and choosing the language to fit each public. It's a masterclass in tailoring image repair, and in reframing to take control of a narrative the media had already written ("the glamorous corrupt politician").

⚖️ Did it work? & why it matters

Whatever the legal outcome, as communication it's a coherent, theory-consistent strategy: humanise, reframe, control the narrative, and meet each audience where it is. The transferable lesson — the same facts can support several truthful frames; pick the one that fits the audience you most need to win. (And note the ethical edge IRT always carries: persuasive ≠ innocent.)

🎯 In the exam

Use Qatargate to prove you understand IRT beyond a list: name the strategies, but make your point the audience-tailoring (legal/political/emotional by country) and the reframing. It pairs beautifully with Carrefour (innocent vs accused) to show the full range of image repair.

＋ The crisis plan — the prep the failures keep proving

You've now seen why preparation matters: BP had no plan; Colectiv's officials contradicted each other. The crisis-plan lecture is simply the checklist that would have prevented those failures. Its purpose: manage communication through one formal channel to protect a reputation of leadership and transparency.

📌 The rules an examiner will reward
  • "No comment" is never acceptable — it reads as guilt. Give a holding statement and a time for the next update.
  • One lead (coordinates everything) + one designated spokesperson (faces the media). Only they speak. (Exactly what Colectiv lacked.)
  • Report immediately · be proactive and transparent · keep personnel matters confidential.
  • Always evaluate afterwards — capture the lessons learned.

Two more pieces worth a line each: the questions people ask first — the public: "Am I safe? What can I do? Who caused this? Can you fix it?"; the media: "What happened? Who's in charge? Why? Did you have forewarning?" — and a named decision tool, Kepner-Tregoe (situation appraisal → decision analysis → problem analysis → potential-problem analysis).

＋ Toolkit recap — now that you've seen them work

You've met every framework inside a story. Here they are in one place, so you can revise fast. Each links back to the case that teaches it.

SCCT — Coombs (lives in: BP)

Match your response to attributed blame (Attribution Theory). Types: victim < accidental < preventable; intensifiers: history + bad reputation. Postures: deny < diminish < rebuild (+ bolstering). Rule: match the posture to the blame and stay consistent.

Apologia & Image Repair — Benoit (lives in: Carrefour, Qatargate)

Apologia = self-defence rhetoric (denial, bolstering, differentiation, transcendence). Benoit's 5 IRT categories: denial · evasion of responsibility · reducing offensiveness · corrective action · mortification. First ask: what's the accusation? who's the audience? Both are descriptive, not evaluative.

EPPM & risk perception — Witte / Slovic (lives in: COVID)

Perception ≠ statistics; fear rises with dread + unknown factors (Slovic) and a "culture of fear" (Furedi). EPPM: pair threat (susceptibility + severity) with efficacy (response + self) → danger control; threat without efficacy → fear control.

The first-message template (lives in: Terror attacks)

For a victim crisis: sympathy → information + measures → appreciation → unity/triumph → invoke values. The "round-the-flag" effect.

Framing — Iyengar (lives in: Colectiv)

Episodic framing (individual events) → individual blame; thematic framing (context/trends) → systemic blame. Whoever frames the story shapes who gets blamed.

The crisis plan (lives in: every failure)

One lead + one spokesperson; never "no comment"; be proactive and consistent; evaluate after. Decision tool: Kepner-Tregoe.

What is a crisis? (the definitions)

Coombs (2007): the perception of an unpredictable event threatening stakeholders' expectancies. Fearn-Banks (1996): a major occurrence with a negative outcome that can threaten the organisation's existence. Public rates you on openness · competence · fairness · empathy.

＋ Now you do it — the exam template

Whatever case you're given, walk this path. It's the same logic you just read six times.

  1. 📰 Describe the crisis — events, actors, who's responsible, the media response.
  2. 🧭 Classify the type (victim/accident/preventable) — did it shift? add intensifiers (history, reputation).
  3. 🗣️ Name the strategy — SCCT posture and/or Benoit's categories, with exact sub-strategies + quotes.
  4. ✅ Check the essentials — empathy? measures? consistency? one spokesperson? aligned with other actors?
  5. 🎲 Risk/EPPM — did the message pair threat with a doable action?
  6. 👥 Audience — what was the public asked to do?
  7. ⚖️ Evaluate — did it work, and what would you change? This is where the marks are.

❓ Practise applying it

A factory explosion is caused by a valve the company knew was faulty but chose not to replace. Classify and prescribe.

Preventable, high blame. Wrong move = deny/diminish (BP's mistake). Right move = rebuild: accept responsibility, corrective action, compensation, one empathetic spokesperson, consistent message.

A celebrity chef is filmed serving undercooked chicken. Give one move from each of Benoit's five categories.

Denial: "the footage is doctored." Evasion: "the oven thermostat failed" (defeasibility). Reducing offensiveness: "one plate of thousands; 20 years of safe kitchens" (minimisation + bolstering). Corrective action: "new temperature checks." Mortification: "I'm sorry — my fault."

Rewrite this failing message with EPPM: "A deadly virus is spreading and there's nothing you can do."

Add efficacy: "This virus spreads fast and can be serious (threat) — wash your hands for 20 seconds and wear a mask; these work and you can do them today (efficacy)." Now it drives danger control, not panic.

Draft the opening of a leader's statement after a terror attack.

Sympathy ("our thoughts are with the victims and their families") → information + measures ("here's what we know and what we're doing") → appreciation ("I thank our emergency services") → unity ("we stand together and will not be divided") → values ("this was an attack on our democracy and freedom").

A TV channel covers a factory fire only through one grieving family. Which framing, and what's the effect?

Episodic (individual story) → audiences assign individual blame and feel emotion, but there's less pressure for systemic accountability. Compare "Corruption Kills" (thematic) after Colectiv.
