Chapter 1

Foundations — what a crisis is

📘 Definitions · dimensions · credibility

📘 The theory

Before any strategy, you need the vocabulary. A crisis has been defined several ways in the course — learn one fully and be able to name the author:

  • Coombs (2007): the perception of an unpredictable event that threatens stakeholders' important expectancies and can seriously impact an organisation's performance. The key word is perception — a crisis is partly in the audience's mind, which is exactly why communication can make or break it.
  • Fearn-Banks (1996): a major occurrence with a potentially negative outcome affecting an organisation and its publics; it interrupts normal business and can threaten the organisation's very existence.
  • Seeger, Sellnow & Ulmer (1998): a specific, unexpected and non-routine event creating high uncertainty and threat to an organisation's high-priority goals.

The components every crisis shares

It is unexpected, poses a threat or challenge, is potentially hazardous, demands immediate, rapid action, occurs where management has limited control, has unpredictable consequences, and inevitably arouses media and public interest.

Crisis communication and its three dimensions

Crisis communication is the exchange of information among authorities, organisations, media and individuals before, during and after a crisis. During a crisis there are three dimensions to manage: (1) the actual crisis itself, (2) the way authorities/organisations handle it, and (3) the resulting crisis image. The lesson hidden here is profound: you can manage the event competently and still lose on image — which is why image (dimension 3) is its own battlefield.

📌 The 4 dimensions of credibility

The public rates an authority's credibility on four things: its openness/honesty, its competence, the fairness of its actions, and the empathy it displays. Treat these four as your scorecard — in any case analysis, ask how the response performed on each.

Finally, the crisis team's opening questions frame everything that follows: What is the nature of the crisis? Who is affected? What is the worst-case scenario? What is at stake? Who else is involved? What will the media and public think and demand? What are the time constraints? What happens after the acute stage?

📂 Case in miniature

Take a company data breach. The actual crisis is the leak; the handling is the investigation and the fix; the image is whether customers still trust you. A firm can patch the breach perfectly (dimension 2) yet be destroyed on dimension 3 if it stays silent or sounds careless — proving the breach is a perception problem as much as a technical one. Every detailed case in this guide is really a study of those three dimensions in tension.

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. Define a crisis and explain why "perception" is central to crisis communication.

Model answer

Cite Coombs (2007): a crisis is the perception of an unpredictable event threatening stakeholders' important expectancies. The word "perception" matters because a crisis is not only what objectively happens but what publics believe is happening; reputation is built on perception, so an organisation can be damaged by how a situation is read even when its actions are technically sound. This is precisely why the three dimensions distinguish the event, its handling and its image — and why communication, which shapes perception, is decisive. (You can support with Fearn-Banks' definition: a negative occurrence that can threaten the organisation's existence.)

Q. What are the three dimensions of a crisis, and how can an organisation "win" one but "lose" another?

Model answer

The three dimensions are the actual crisis, the handling of it, and the crisis image. An organisation can manage the event well operationally (handling) yet lose on image if it communicates poorly — silence, arrogance, or inconsistency turns competent handling into a perceived failure. Example: BP eventually cleaned up and paid out (handling), but its blame-shifting and a tone-deaf CEO destroyed its image, costing ~25% of its market value. The lesson: managing the crisis and managing the image are separate jobs.

Q. Explain the four dimensions on which the public judges an authority's credibility.

Model answer

Openness/honesty — is the authority transparent and truthful, or hiding/spinning? Competence — does it seem capable of managing the situation? Fairness — are its actions just and proportionate to all affected? Empathy — does it show genuine concern for victims and the public? These four explain many failures: BP scored badly on openness (censoring information), competence (underestimating the leak) and empathy ("I'd like my life back"); the Queen's COVID address scored highly on empathy and competence (calm, reassuring), which is why it is held up as a model.

Chapter 2

SCCT — Situational Crisis Communication Theory

📘 W. Timothy Coombs (2007) · 📂 case: BP oil spill

📘 The theory

SCCT is the field's central reputation-management theory. Its one big idea: match your crisis response to how much responsibility the public attributes to you, and to the reputational threat you face. It is built on Attribution Theory — when something bad happens, people automatically search for a cause to blame; SCCT tells you to read that blame and respond proportionately.

Step 1 — Assess the reputational threat (4 factors)

  1. Crisis type — sets the baseline responsibility (below).
  2. Severity — how much damage/harm was done.
  3. History — has the organisation had similar crises before?
  4. Prior reputation / relationship — was the existing relationship with stakeholders good or bad?

The last two double as the two intensifying factors: a history of crises and a prior negative reputation both raise attributed responsibility. The same event lands harder on an organisation with baggage.

Step 2 — The three crisis types (rising blame)

ClusterResponsibilityExamples
VictimMinimalnatural disaster, rumour, product tampering, workplace violence, malevolence
AccidentalLowtechnical-error accidents, challenges
PreventableStronghuman-error accidents, organisational misdeeds, management misconduct

Step 3 — The response strategies

Coombs groups responses into three rising postures, plus a secondary support set:

  • DENY — attack the accuser (confront whoever claims a crisis exists), denial (assert there is no crisis), scapegoat (blame someone outside the organisation).
  • DIMINISH — excuse (deny intent to do harm / minimise responsibility), justification (minimise the perceived damage).
  • REBUILD — compensation (money or gifts to victims), apology (take full responsibility and ask forgiveness).
  • BOLSTERING (secondary, supports the others) — reminder (recall past good works), ingratiation (praise stakeholders), victimage (note the organisation is a victim too).

Underpinning all of this is "informing and adjusting information": in every crisis you first give people the facts and tell them how to cope. For low-responsibility victim crises with no history, that base level plus deny/diminish can be enough.

📌 The matching rule (the exam's favourite)

Low blame (victim) → informing/adjusting + deny or diminish. Low-but-real blame (accident) → diminish, sometimes rebuild. High blame (preventable), or any case with intensifying factors → rebuild (compensation, apology, corrective action). And whatever you choose, maintain consistency — Coombs' rule that a shifting story reads as deception or loss of control.

📂 Case study: BP & the Deepwater Horizon oil spill (2010)

What happened. On 20 April 2010 the Deepwater Horizon rig — owned by Transocean, leased by BP — exploded in the Gulf of Mexico. Eleven workers died; about 4.9 million barrels of oil spilled, the largest in US history. By June, 1,100 miles of coastline were polluted. BP set up a $20bn compensation fund under presidential pressure, lost roughly 25% of its market value by mid-2011, and faced a final bill near $61.6bn (2016).

Read the crisis. Type = preventable (operational/judgment failures BP could control → high attributed blame); severity = extreme; history = prior safety incidents (intensifier); reputation = already weak on safety/environment (intensifier). Verdict of the situation: this is a maximum-blame case that requires the rebuild posture.

What BP did (the mismatch). Instead, BP and CEO Tony Hayward used the low-blame playbook:

"It wasn't our accident… It was their rig, their equipment, their people."→ shifting blame / scapegoat (DENY)
"The Gulf of Mexico is a very big ocean… the amount of oil is tiny" · "the environmental impact… is likely to be very, very modest."→ minimisation (DIMINISH)
"There's no one who wants this over more than I do. I'd like my life back."→ the spokesperson makes himself the victim — tone-deaf

And a "disclaimer" diffused blame ("a complex accident… multiple parties… not up to us to say who is at fault"). Off-stage it was worse: BP tried to limit media access, offered plaintiffs $5,000 to drop suits, had no crisis plan, and violated its own spill plan (which forbade promising restoration). It eventually pivoted to rebuild — the $20bn fund, cleanup websites, a research initiative, "We will make this right" ads.

Verdict. A high-blame crisis answered with deny/diminish is a textbook mismatch; combined with a shifting story and a self-pitying CEO, it converted public anger into a 640,000-strong "Boycott BP" group and a satirical @BPGlobalPR account with ten times the followers of BP's real account. BP lost ~25% of its value.

💡 Relevancy + the mirror image

BP proves SCCT in the negative: when you own the crisis, denial adds the charges of arrogance and dishonesty. The right response was rebuild + one empathetic, consistent spokesperson. Contrast it with BP's 2025 shareholder revolt — an accidental/management crisis (a strategy U-turn, no misconduct), where the correct match was diminish + corrective action with no apology ("we went too far, too fast"; leadership transition). Same company, different type, opposite correct response — that is the theory.

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. Explain SCCT and how a crisis manager selects an appropriate response strategy.

Model answer

SCCT (Coombs, 2007), grounded in Attribution Theory, holds that managers should match their response to the responsibility stakeholders attribute. The process: (1) assess the reputational threat through four factors — type, severity, history, prior reputation; (2) classify the crisis as victim, accidental or preventable; (3) choose a posture — deny (denial, attack accuser, scapegoat) for rumour/victim crises; diminish (excuse, justification) for low responsibility; rebuild (compensation, apology) for high responsibility or when intensifying factors (history, bad reputation) are present — always layered on informing/adjusting information and supported by bolstering; (4) maintain consistency. The art is in the match: too little accommodation reads as arrogance, too much as guilt. BP illustrates the failure of a mismatch (high blame met with deny/diminish).

Q. A pharmaceutical company learns a contaminated batch — caused by a supplier's error it had been warned about — has reached patients. Classify and prescribe a response.

Model answer

Because the company was warned, this sits between accidental and preventable — attributed responsibility is high, and the health severity is high, so the reputational threat is serious. SCCT prescribes the rebuild posture: lead with instructing/adjusting information (recall, what patients should do), then apology/mortification, compensation, and corrective action (new supplier audits). Deny/diminish would be a dangerous mismatch. Use one consistent, empathetic spokesperson; support with bolstering (the company's safety record) but never let it look like an excuse. Evaluate as it unfolds.

Q. Why did BP's communication fail in SCCT terms? Use specific strategies.

Model answer

BP faced a preventable, high-severity crisis with intensifying factors (safety history, weak reputation) — a maximum-blame situation requiring rebuild. Instead it used deny (shifting blame to Transocean, a disclaimer diffusing fault) and diminish (minimising the spill's impact) — a clear mismatch. It also broke the consistency rule (the story shifted from "not ours" to "we'll make it right") and chose a poor spokesperson (Hayward's "I'd like my life back" centred himself, not the victims). The result confirmed SCCT: under-accommodation in a high-blame crisis reads as arrogance and dishonesty, fuelling boycotts and a 25% value loss.

Q. What are the "intensifying factors" and why do they change the recommended response?

Model answer

They are a history of similar crises and a prior negative reputation. Both raise the responsibility stakeholders attribute, because a pattern or a bad track record makes the public read even an "accident" as negligence. So a crisis that might warrant only diminish on its own should be escalated to rebuild when intensifiers are present — the organisation has less benefit of the doubt and must be more accommodative to protect its reputation.

Chapter 3

Apologia & Image Repair Theory

📘 Ware & Linkugel / Benoit (1995) · 📂 case: Carrefour in China

📘 The theory

This is the family of theories about defending and repairing a damaged image. SCCT asks "how much are you to blame?"; these ask "what exactly is the accusation, and how do you talk your way back to a good image?"

Apologia

Apologia is the rhetoric of self-defence (Greek apologia; crucially, it does not mean "apology"). It originates in political communication as four strategies for someone under attack for wrongdoing:

  1. Denial — claim no involvement.
  2. Bolstering — divert attention to the accused's good deeds.
  3. Differentiation — remove the act from its negative context (re-describe it, compare it to something worse).
  4. Transcendence — place the act in a broader, more favourable context.

Corporate apologia

Applied to organisations, apologia offers three routes: conciliation (acknowledge guilt, apologise, add compensation/corrective action); redefinition (diffuse the public's perception of guilt); and dissociation (separate the organisation from the crisis).

  • Redefinition sub-strategies: provocation (we reacted to hostilities), defeasibility (superior forces; nothing could be done), good intentions (acted in good faith, unexpected results), minimisation (the problem is small).
  • Dissociation forms: opinion–knowledge ("people think we're linked, but facts say otherwise"), individual–group ("one person/unit is responsible, not the whole organisation"), accident–essence ("it happened and we're responsible, but it's an isolated incident that doesn't represent us").

Image Repair Theory (Benoit, 1995)

Benoit systematised and expanded apologia into the most widely used image-defence framework. Two foundations: an attack has two parts — an offensive act + an accusation of responsibility (remove either and there's no threat); and you must first answer two questions — What is the accusation? Who is the key audience? (audiences ultimately validate or reject what you project). The five categories:

  1. Denial — simple denial · shifting the blame.
  2. Evasion of responsibility — provocation · defeasibility (lack of information/control) · accident · good intentions.
  3. Reducing offensiveness — bolstering · minimisation · differentiation · transcendence · attacking the accuser · compensation.
  4. Corrective action — fix the problem and/or prevent recurrence.
  5. Mortification — admit responsibility and apologise.
⚠️ Strengths & the honest limitation

IRT is comprehensive, adaptable and excellent for training spokespeople. But apologia and IRT are descriptive, not evaluative — they name the moves but don't prove which one works (that's left to the practitioner). And used cynically, IRT can help a guilty organisation evade accountability — its ethical weak spot.

📂 Case study: the Carrefour boycott in China (2008)

Context. 2008 — Beijing was hosting the Olympics (huge national prestige). Abroad, human-rights groups protested China's Tibet policy and attacked Olympic sponsors; when the torch passed through Paris, protesters scuffled with a wheelchair-using Chinese athlete carrying the flame. Chinese citizens were furious. Then a false rumour spread that the French chain Carrefour was financing the Dalai Lama and supporting Tibetan independence. A boycott formed online and then physically, with damage to stores. Carrefour had done nothing — pure guilt by association.

Read it & the strategy. Carrefour is a victim of a false accusation, so the question is image repair: kill the rumour in the eyes of the Chinese public. Its first move was a mistake — a week of silence — but it then executed well:

  • Denial (cat. 1) — flatly rejected the pro-Tibet link.
  • Adjusting information + sympathy — expressed concern for those affected.
  • Ingratiation (cat. 3) — praised the Beijing Olympics and China.
  • A 3-step rumour-debunk: acknowledge why people might believe it → explain why it's untrue → state the truth (its policy of staying out of politics and religion).
"Carrefour is innocent, but we understand the feeling of the Chinese people… [we] never did and will not do anything concerned with politics and religion."Jean-Luc Lhuillier, VP Carrefour China — denial + sympathy + ingratiation

A third actor mattered: the Chinese government, conflicted between national pride and its Olympic image, discouraged the anti-French protests and urged "rational" patriotism ("the best way to love your country is to do your job well").

💡 Relevancy

When you are innocent but accused, the danger is the accusation, not an act — so respond fast (silence lets a rumour harden into truth), debunk in three honest steps, and align with what the audience values (here, the Olympics and national pride). It is also the clean counterpart to a guilty case — compare it with Qatargate (Ch.8).

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. Compare Apologia and Image Repair Theory. What are the strengths and limits of each?

Model answer

Apologia is the older, rhetorical theory of self-defence, naming four moves (denial, bolstering, differentiation, transcendence) and, for organisations, three routes (conciliation, redefinition, dissociation). Benoit's IRT systematised and expanded this into five categories (denial, evasion of responsibility, reducing offensiveness, corrective action, mortification) with many sub-strategies, adding the crucial emphasis on the audience and on corrective action and mortification as repair options. Strengths: comprehensive, adaptable, useful for training spokespeople. Limits shared by both: they are descriptive, not evaluative (no proof of effectiveness), and IRT can be misused to help guilty actors dodge accountability. Illustrate with Carrefour (innocent: denial + reducing offensiveness) and Colectiv/Qatargate (accused: shifting blame, transcendence, victimage).

Q. A restaurant chain is filmed with rats in its kitchen. Construct a response using one strategy from each of Benoit's five categories, and say which you'd actually choose and why.

Model answer

Denial: "that footage isn't from our kitchen." Evasion (defeasibility): "a city-wide infestation we couldn't have foreseen." Reducing offensiveness (minimisation + bolstering): "an isolated branch; 500 outlets with top hygiene scores." Corrective action: "immediate deep-clean, independent inspections, results published." Mortification: "we are sorry; this falls far below our standards."
What I'd choose: since the act is real and visible, denial and evasion would be a dangerous mismatch (and break consistency). The credible path is mortification + corrective action, supported lightly by bolstering — accept it, fix it visibly, prove it won't recur. This also satisfies the credibility dimensions of honesty and competence.

Q. Define and distinguish: defeasibility, transcendence, differentiation, mortification.

Model answer

Defeasibility (evasion of responsibility): plead lack of information or control — superior forces meant nothing could be done. Transcendence (reducing offensiveness): reframe the act within a bigger, more favourable context (e.g. "all of Europe faced the same shortage"). Differentiation (reducing offensiveness): take the act out of its negative context by comparing it to something worse, so it seems less bad. Mortification: the opposite of all of these — fully admit responsibility and apologise. The first three reduce perceived responsibility/offensiveness; mortification accepts it.

Q. Analyse Carrefour's response to the 2008 boycott using image-repair concepts.

Model answer

Carrefour was a victim of guilt by association — a false rumour (accusation without a real offensive act), so the task was image repair aimed at the Chinese public. Its mistake was a week of silence that let the rumour harden. Its recovery combined denial (category 1) with reducing offensiveness (category 3) via ingratiation (praising the Olympics and China) and a disciplined 3-step rumour-debunk. It showed cultural empathy ("we understand the feeling of the Chinese people"), aligning with what the audience valued. The case shows IRT's logic — identify the accusation, identify the audience, and choose strategies that the audience will validate — and the cost of delay.

Chapter 4

The Crisis Communication Plan

📘 Preparation · policy · decision-making

📘 The theory

Purpose. A plan manages communication through a formal, clearly defined channel to mitigate the crisis and maintain a reputation of leadership and transparency. It prepares staff to respond in a unified, professional way and to control the flow of sensitive information.

The policy — rules that turn up in exams

  • Report every crisis to the supervisor/CEO immediately.
  • Only the designated spokesperson (and back-ups) release information to media/public.
  • There is one crisis-management lead (coordinates everything, manages messages and media) and one spokesperson (the person who actually faces the media).
  • All comments are guided by professionalism and transparency.
  • "No comment" is never an acceptable response.
  • Personnel matters stay confidential; responses should be proactive, responsive, action-oriented.

The team & the checklist

The ideal team: CEO, PR/Communications, senior advisors/department heads, Finance, Government Relations, HR, Investor Relations, Operations, Safety, and Legal counsel. The checklist runs: Safety (people and site) → Notification (CEO at once) → convene the team → Before Going Public (assign lead + spokesperson, establish facts, decide the response, draft messages, prepare talking points) → Going Public (brief internal audiences first, then media; update website/voicemail) → Evaluate message effectiveness as it unfolds and afterward.

What people ask first

The public: "Am I and my family safe? What affects me? What can I do to protect us? Who caused this? Can you fix it?" The media: "What happened? Who's in charge? Is it contained? Are victims helped? Why did this happen? Did you have forewarning?" A good first statement anticipates these.

Decision-making — Kepner-Tregoe

A named methodology for structured decisions under pressure: situation appraisal (what is going on?), decision analysis (what is the best alternative?), problem analysis (what went wrong?), and potential-problem analysis (what could go wrong?). Channel choice scales with urgency: newsletter (low) → one-on-one meetings → media release → media conference (high urgency, big issue).

📂 Case study: what happens without a plan

The plan's value is proven by the cases that lacked it. BP (Ch.2) "has not prepared a crisis communication plan," improvised under pressure, blamed third parties before acting, and even broke its own filed spill plan — the textbook cost of no preparation. Colectiv (Ch.7) shows the specific failure of the spokesperson rule: Secretary of State Arafat and the Ministry of Health gave press statements that contradicted each other, so the single-voice principle collapsed and credibility leaked. A classroom exercise from the course drives it home: you are a school principal who receives an anonymous threat that a gunman will come today — write the media statement and the email to families. The plan tells you exactly how: confirm safety first, designate the spokesperson, state the facts and the measures, never say "no comment," and give a time for the next update.

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. Why is "No comment" never acceptable, and what should an organisation say instead before all facts are known?

Model answer

"No comment" reads as evasion or guilt and cedes the narrative to others — it fails the credibility dimensions of openness and competence. Instead, issue a holding statement: confirm what is known and verified, express genuine concern for those affected, describe the immediate measures being taken, and commit to when the next update will come. This satisfies the public's first questions ("am I safe? can you fix it?") and the media's ("who's in charge?") while protecting accuracy, and it keeps the organisation as the authoritative source.

Q. Outline a crisis communication response for a chemical leak at a factory near a residential area.

Model answer

Follow the checklist: (1) Safety — secure the site, support evacuation. (2) Notify the CEO and convene the team (incl. Safety, Legal, Operations, Gov Relations). (3) Before going public — designate one lead + one spokesperson, establish the facts, decide the response. (4) Going public — brief internal staff and authorities first, then a media release/conference (high urgency). Content uses the first-message logic: concern for residents → what happened and the measures (evacuation zone, air monitoring) → what people should do (stay indoors / leave) → who is in charge → next update time. Pair the threat with a doable action (EPPM). Never "no comment"; maintain one consistent voice across the company and local authorities. (5) Evaluate and update continuously.

Q. Why does the plan insist on a single spokesperson and a single lead? Illustrate the cost of ignoring it.

Model answer

A single spokesperson and lead guarantee one consistent, professional message and clear coordination — essential because consistency is what makes a response credible (Coombs' rule). Multiple voices risk contradiction, which signals confusion or dishonesty and erodes trust. Colectiv shows the cost: Arafat and the Health Ministry made statements that differed, undermining both. The principle protects the credibility dimensions of competence and honesty.

Chapter 5

The first message after a terror attack

📘 The consolation script · 📂 case: Nice, Brussels, London, Barcelona

📘 The theory

A terror attack inflicts deaths and destruction and deliberately manufactures panic, fear and division. We live in a "risk society" poorly equipped to deal with risk (Bakker), within a media-driven "culture of fear" (Furedi) — which makes the first official message extraordinarily powerful. Recovery depends on a planned strategy aimed at three audiences at once: the media, the general public, and the authorities' own internal staff.

📌 The five-part first-message template
  1. Express sympathy for victims and their families.
  2. Give information on what happened and the measures being taken.
  3. Show appreciation for the emergency services and police.
  4. Emphasise unity and triumph over the threat — the "round-the-flag" effect.
  5. Invoke the values the attack targeted — democracy, liberty, human rights, unity.

Because the nation is a victim, there is no blame to deny — so the message is not about defence but about meaning and morale: deny the attackers their goal (fear and division) by replacing panic with solidarity. The differences between leaders are the analytical payoff: the degree of emotion, how the call to action is framed, how the country's history of attacks is read, and what is expected of citizens.

📂 Case study: four European attacks (2016–17)

Nice (14 July 2016, 86 dead), Brussels (22 March 2016, 32), London Bridge (3 June 2017, 7), Barcelona (17 Aug 2017, 13). The course finds the same five-part structure in all four leaders' statements, with revealing differences in emphasis:

"What we feared has happened… I call for calm and solidarity… we have to all face this unified as one."Charles Michel (Brussels) — calm, solidarity, unity above all
"The police responded with great courage and great speed… I want to thank and pay tribute to the professionalism and bravery of the police and the emergency services."Theresa May (London) — appreciation + round-the-flag; plus a 4-step plan and "enough is enough" (during an election)

Hollande (Nice) used heavy negative-emotion words ("horror", "massacre") and turned outward to military action in Syria and Iraq ("France… is strong and it will always be stronger"). Rajoy (Barcelona) put victims first and framed terrorism globally ("Madrid, Paris, Nice, Brussels, Berlin and London have all felt the same pain"). All reaffirmed the same fundamental values; they differed in tone (Hollande most emotive, Michel most restrained) and in the call to action (May most political and security-focused).

💡 Relevancy

In a blameless crisis your job is consolation and unity, not defence. Lead with the people, not the policy. This is the same template behind your own US–Iran presentation's "first message" point — where you argued the US skipped the consolation script and opened with victory and threat, the opposite of what these four leaders did.

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. What should the first official message to the public after a terror attack contain? Support with the cases.

Model answer

It addresses three audiences (media, public, internal staff) and follows a five-part structure: (1) sympathy for victims and families; (2) information on events and measures; (3) appreciation for emergency services and police; (4) unity and triumph over the threat (round-the-flag); (5) invocation of the values the attack targeted (democracy, liberty, human rights). All four European cases share this skeleton — e.g. Michel (Brussels) "calm and solidarity… unified as one"; May (London) thanking the police; Hollande (Nice) reaffirming France's strength. The differences lie in emotion, the call to action and the reading of national history. The aim is to deny the attackers their goal — fear and division — by replacing panic with solidarity.

Q. Compare how two leaders responded to terror attacks.

Model answer

May (London Bridge) vs Hollande (Nice). Both followed the template (sympathy, information, appreciation, unity, values), but May was political and security-focused — she praised the police, set out a four-point counter-terror plan, and used the line "enough is enough" during an election. Hollande was more emotive ("horror", "massacre") and turned outward to military action in Syria and Iraq, framing the attack as a strike on French values met with strength. The contrast shows the same script can carry different politics: a domestic-reform emphasis (May) versus an emotional, external-response emphasis (Hollande).

Q. Draft the opening of a Prime Minister's statement after a major attack, and justify each part.

Model answer

"Our thoughts are with the victims and their families tonight (1 — sympathy). Here is what we know, and the measures we have taken to keep you safe (2 — information + measures). I want to thank our emergency services and police for their courage (3 — appreciation). We stand together; this country will not be divided by terror (4 — unity / round-the-flag). This was an attack on our democracy, our freedom and our way of life — values we will always defend (5 — values)." Each element either consoles, informs, builds morale, or denies the attackers their goal of fear and division.

Chapter 6

Risk perception & EPPM

📘 Slovic · Furedi · Witte · 📂 case: COVID-19

📘 The theory

The core insight: communicating risk is not the same as giving people data. Risk perception explains why people fear what they fear — and people are disproportionately afraid of spectacular hazards they're statistically safe from, while under-fearing common ones. "People respond to the hazards they perceive" (Slovic, Fischhoff & Lichtenstein, 1979); and the more uncertainty a risk poses, the more it is feared.

Furedi — the "culture of fear" / "risk society": the media over-expose the worst-case and exaggerate the scale of threats; trust in experts and authorities declines; suspicion of hidden interests rises. This primes the public for fear.

Three research directions

  1. Psychometric (Slovic): perceived risk rises with two factor-families — Dread (uncontrollable, catastrophic, fatal, inequitable, involuntary, not-easily-reduced, increasing) and the Unknown (not observable, effects delayed, new, unknown to science). High dread + high unknown = high perceived risk, regardless of statistics.
  2. Cultural Theory of Risk (Douglas): risk attitudes compared on two dimensions — grid (the roles, control and stratification people accept) and group (the strength of bonds between them). The word "risk" is preferred to "danger" in political discourse because it carries the aura of science and "looks forward".
  3. Social Amplification of Risk: information processes, institutions and social groups amplify or attenuate risk signals as they pass through society (filter/decode → process → attach values → validate with peers → form intentions → act).
🧪 EPPM — Extended Parallel Process Model (Kim Witte)

A model for effective public-health / crisis messages. A message works when people follow the recommended action — danger control. Two pairs of perceptions decide this: the threat — perceived susceptibility ("could this affect me?") and perceived severity ("how bad is it?"); and the efficacy — response/plan efficacy ("does the recommended action work?") and self-efficacy ("can I do it?"). The rule: high threat must be paired with high efficacy. Threat with no workable action pushes people into fear control — denial, avoidance, switching off.

📂 Case study: COVID-19 (UK, France, Romania)

COVID is the course's "everything at once" case: a long, frightening, ongoing crisis where the whole task was to persuade millions to change behaviour. Three governments did it differently.

🇬🇧 United Kingdom — two voices

Boris Johnson: informal (sing "Happy Birthday" twice while washing hands), non-apologetic, overestimated the country's readiness. Analysts caught him using justification, transcendence and differentiation ("British exceptionalism"), and EPPM's increased severity/susceptibility. By contrast, Queen Elizabeth II's 5 April 2020 address is the course's model: calm, rational, anchored in unity and "national spirit", appealing to pride and connecting past–present–future; she pointedly avoided the words "pandemic", "coronavirus" and "crisis", and made no promises about remedies.

🇫🇷 France — accessibility as strategy

France made information reach everyone: a WhatsApp chatbot, the TousAntiCovid app, the Désinfox fact-checking service, easy-read FALC documents with pictograms, and material in 24 languages plus sign-language-interpreted briefings. PM Castex ("Monsieur Déconfinement") was accessible but stumbled (blamed citizens; admitted not downloading the app). His 14 Jan 2021 speech is a clinic in the toolkit: transcendence ("all of Europe faces successive waves"), differentiation (France's results "more than honourable"), ingratiation/SCCT (thanking citizens and teachers), and EPPM (focus on severity + susceptibility, then self/response efficacy — our measures work, keep following them). President Macron used warrior rhetoric — "a first victory against the virus", patriotism, a tone of national superiority.

🇷🇴 Romania — good intent, poor delivery

Emergency decrees announced at 10–11 p.m.; specialist jargon; PM Cîțu rushed and mispronounced (even the tracing app's name). The government had to fight a top-ten list of vaccine myths ("vaccination will be compulsory", "politicians will get it first", etc.), and leaned more on warnings than on thanking compliant citizens (transcendence + differentiation: "we are in Europe, in the top three"). Arafat — the Colectiv official, carrying a prior negative reputation — returned as a trusted-but-prickly information source who criticised media pressure on medical staff.

💡 Relevancy

COVID shows every theory operating together — Coombs (justification, ingratiation), Benoit (transcendence, differentiation) and above all Witte's EPPM — and it proves the central rule: leaders succeeded by pairing realistic threat with clear, doable action (and, like the Queen, with calm and shared identity); they failed when they scared without efficacy, blamed citizens, or delivered badly.

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. Explain the EPPM and use it to fix a failing public-health message.

Model answer

Witte's EPPM says a fear message works only if threat (perceived susceptibility + severity) is matched with efficacy (response efficacy + self-efficacy); then people enter danger control and act. Without efficacy they default to fear control — denial, avoidance. Failing message: "A deadly virus is spreading and there's nothing you can do" — high threat, zero efficacy → panic or fatalism. Fixed: "This virus spreads fast and can be serious (susceptibility + severity); washing your hands for 20 seconds and wearing a mask significantly reduce your risk, and you can do them today (response + self-efficacy)." Now the threat motivates action rather than denial.

Q. Why do people fear terrorism or air travel more than far deadlier everyday risks? Use risk-perception theory.

Model answer

Because perceived risk is driven by perception, not statistics (Slovic). Terrorism and plane crashes score high on Slovic's dread factors (uncontrollable, catastrophic, involuntary, fatal) and on the unknown (unpredictable, dramatic), so they feel far riskier than driving, which is familiar, voluntary and individually controllable. Furedi's "culture of fear" amplifies this: media over-expose the worst case and trust in experts falls. Social amplification then spreads the signal through institutions and peer groups. The communication implication: address the perception, and pair any threat with a workable action (EPPM).

Q. Compare the COVID communication of two leaders, naming the strategies each used.

Model answer

Queen Elizabeth II vs Boris Johnson. The Queen's 5 April 2020 address is the model: calm, rational, anchored in unity and national spirit, appealing to pride, deliberately avoiding alarming words ("pandemic", "crisis") and making no false promises — high on the credibility dimensions of empathy and competence. Johnson was informal and non-apologetic, overestimated readiness, and leaned on justification, transcendence and differentiation ("British exceptionalism") plus EPPM severity/susceptibility — effective at conveying threat but weaker on reassurance and consistency. The contrast shows two registers: reassurance and shared identity (Queen) versus threat-management without accountability (Johnson).

Chapter 7

Health & security crisis — Colectiv & framing

📘 Public-sector comms · framing (Iyengar) · 📂 case: Colectiv

📘 The theory

This chapter applies SCCT and Benoit to a public-sector crisis and adds two ideas the exam likes.

Public-sector specifics

Public institutions face two facets — the crisis itself and its perception — and the course's lesson is that they must take social-media interaction seriously (not just one-way posting), be open to consulting communication experts from the private sector, and remember that political dimensions shape the response. Crucially, the rule of one consistent voice applies just as much here as in companies.

Framing — episodic vs thematic (Shanto Iyengar)

How the media frame a crisis decides whom the public blames. Episodic framing tells the story through specific events and individuals → audiences assign individual responsibility (and feel emotion). Thematic framing sets the story in context, trends and systems → audiences assign societal/systemic responsibility. The frame is therefore a political force: it can keep blame on one person, or escalate it to "the system".

📂 Case study: the Colectiv club fire (2015)

What happened. On 30 October 2015, indoor pyrotechnics at the Colectiv club in Bucharest set the foam ceiling alight; the toll rose from 27 to 65 as burn victims died over weeks — many because Romania's burn-care capacity was overwhelmed and patients had to be transferred abroad. Public fury at corruption and lax safety enforcement produced mass protests, and within days the Prime Minister (Ponta), his government and the local mayor (Piedone) resigned. (This is the professor's own published research — know it in detail.)

Read it (Coombs). Type = accident bordering on preventable (a foreseeable safety failure); severity = very high (health, life); no direct prior history; no specific prior negative relationship. Responsibility was shared and contested between club owners, inspectors and institutions — so the communication became a fight over who is to blame.

Three communication failures (Benoit + the plan):

  • Club owners — shifting blame. They accepted only partial responsibility and pointed at inspectors and authorities ("I have my share of responsibility, but I'd like it defined correctly… what institutions made mistakes"). → denial / shifting blame + evading responsibility.
  • Raed Arafat — competent but cold. The emergency chief explained patient transfers and international help (Israel, France, UK, Norway, Finland) and used transcendence (burn-care capacity is limited across Europe, not just Romania), but showed little empathy, drowned the message in medical detail, and attacked the accusers — the TV "experts" criticising the response ("the doctor talks without knowing how the systems work"). Defensive, not consoling.
  • The institutions — clashing voices. Fast press releases were a plus, but Arafat's and the Health Ministry's statements contradicted each other; and the authorities largely ignored social media as a channel for interaction (the official page posted information but didn't engage — one post had 99 shares and a single reply).

Framing & outcome. The civic movement "Corupția ucide" (Corruption Kills) used heavily thematic framing — pinning the fire on systemic corruption rather than one club owner — which is exactly why it fed political consequences. As crisis communication the state's response largely failed (thin empathy, clashing voices, ignored social media); as a political event the thematic frame won, the government fell, and President Iohannis took the unusual step of consulting civil society.

💡 Relevancy

Two lessons: being factually right isn't enough — Arafat was often correct but emotionally absent, and correctness without empathy or consistency still loses trust; and whoever frames the story shapes the blame — episodic vs thematic framing is how a single fire became a national reckoning. Colectiv is your best "use four tools at once" case (Coombs + Benoit + the plan + Iyengar).

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. Define episodic and thematic framing (Iyengar) and apply them to the Colectiv crisis.

Model answer

Episodic framing presents an issue through specific events or individuals, leading audiences to attribute individual responsibility and respond emotionally; thematic framing sets it in broader context and trends, leading audiences to attribute systemic responsibility. At Colectiv, framing the fire episodically (one negligent club owner) would have contained blame; instead the civic movement "Corupția ucide" framed it thematically as systemic corruption and weak state enforcement. That thematic frame mobilised mass protest and produced political consequences — the resignation of the Prime Minister, government and mayor. The case shows framing as a decisive force in directing public blame.

Q. Analyse the public authorities' communication during Colectiv using SCCT and Image Repair.

Model answer

By SCCT the crisis was high-severity, accident/preventable, with contested responsibility — calling for empathetic, consistent, accommodative communication. By Benoit: the club owners used denial / shifting blame and evasion; Arafat used transcendence (Europe-wide burn-care limits) and attacking the accuser (the TV "experts"), with little empathy. The decisive failures were of the crisis-plan kind: Arafat and the Ministry of Health gave contradictory statements (breaking the single-voice rule), and the state used social media only to post, not to interact. The result: competent medical handling but a damaged image, amplified by thematic media framing — and ultimately a political collapse.

Q. Why did the public-sector response to Colectiv struggle compared with a private-company response?

Model answer

Several reasons the course highlights: responsibility was diffuse and political (owners, inspectors, ministries), so no single actor "owned" the message; officials contradicted each other, breaking consistency; the lead voice (Arafat) was technically competent but emotionally distant and defensive; and the institutions under-used social media as a two-way channel. The lesson drawn is that the public sector should be more open to private-sector communication expertise and treat social-media interaction as essential — because a crisis has two facets, the event and its perception, and the perception was lost.

Chapter 8

Political / institutional crisis — Qatargate

📘 Audience-tailored image repair · 📂 case: Eva Kaili

📘 The theory

This chapter pushes Image Repair Theory to its most sophisticated use and answers Benoit's second question — who is the audience? — in the strongest way. The strategies are the familiar IRT menu (denial, justification, transcendence, dissociation, victimage, equivocation), but the lesson is about tailoring and reframing:

  • Audience-centred strategy. The same accusation can be answered with a different mix for different publics — what persuades one audience may alienate another.
  • Reframing. When the media have already written your story ("the glamorous corrupt politician"), image repair means offering an alternative frame the audience can adopt.
  • Layered, timed communication. A coordinated sequence of messages across channels, languages and moments — controlling not just what is said but when and to whom.
  • Equivocation — answering incompletely on the most dangerous points (e.g. others' involvement) while appearing responsive.

📂 Case study: Eva Kaili's three interviews (2023)

What happened. Qatargate was a corruption scandal in the European Parliament; MEP Eva Kaili — previously a polished, prominent figure — was arrested in December 2022 over alleged ties to Qatar. After months in detention, in June 2023 she launched a deliberate counter-offensive: three newspaper interviews, in three countries, in three languages, within days. The striking feature is that she defended herself differently for each audience.

The pattern. The course calls it a layered strategic communication pattern — legal (France), political (Spain), emotional (Italy) — with the timing of the broadcasts deliberately scheduled to reach a wide range of audiences. The aim throughout: offer an alternative to the "glamorous corrupt politician" portrayal, humanise herself, and reframe and control the narrative. As she put it: "when there is a media storm against you, you have to sit and wait for it to pass before you speak."

💡 Relevancy

The transferable lesson: the same facts can support several defensible frames; choose the one that fits the audience you most need to win. Qatargate is the advanced counterpart to Carrefour — Carrefour was innocent and simply needed to kill a rumour fast; Kaili was accused and needed to reframe a story the media had already set. Together they show the full range of image repair. (And note IRT's ethical edge: a persuasive defence is not proof of innocence.)

❓ Potential exam questions

Q. How did Eva Kaili tailor her image-repair strategy to different audiences? Why is this significant for IRT?

Model answer

Facing one accusation, Kaili ran three interviews with three different frames: legal in France (Libération — judicial injustice, mistreatment in detention), political in Spain (El Mundo — Pegasus surveillance conspiracy), and emotional in Italy (Corriere — motherhood and coercion). She mixed denial, justification ("gifts of equal value"), dissociation/defeasibility, transcendence and victimage according to each public, and timed the releases to maximise reach. This matters for IRT because it dramatises Benoit's second question — who is the audience? — showing that strategy choice is audience-dependent: the right mix is the one a given audience will validate. It is also a case of reframing to wrest the narrative away from the media's "glamorous corrupt politician" portrayal.

Q. Analyse Kaili's defence using Benoit's categories, with examples.

Model answer

Denial — asserting no country offered her money. Reducing offensiveness — justification ("souvenirs of equal value", reframing bribery as diplomacy), transcendence ("don't criminalise diplomatic relations and political work"), and bolstering/reminder (her diligent work as an MEP defending privacy). Evasion of responsibility — defeasibility (lack of knowledge/control over critical facts) and dissociation (distancing herself from Panzeri and others). Victimage — casting herself as the victim of injustice and of her daughter being used against her. Equivocation — answering incompletely on dangerous points (others' involvement). The defence is theory-consistent and coherent — though, as IRT warns, persuasiveness is not evidence of innocence.

Q. Contrast Carrefour and Kaili as image-repair cases. What does each teach?

Model answer

Carrefour was a victim of a false rumour (accusation without a real act); the job was to kill the rumour fast with denial + reducing offensiveness (ingratiation) and a 3-step debunk, aligning with the Chinese audience's values — its lesson is speed and audience-empathy, and the cost of its week of silence. Kaili was an accused figure with a hostile media narrative; she couldn't simply deny, so she reframed and tailored her defence to three audiences (legal/political/emotional) — her lesson is the audience-dependence and narrative control of image repair. Together they map IRT's full range: defending innocence vs repairing a damaged, contested reputation.

🧩 How to write a case answer

Whatever case you're given, walk this path — it mirrors the professor's own analysis checklist and earns marks in order:

  1. Describe the crisis — events, actors, who is responsible, the media response.
  2. Classify the type (victim / accident / preventable) — did it shift? add the intensifiers (history, reputation).
  3. Name the strategy — SCCT posture and/or Benoit's categories, with exact sub-strategies and quotes.
  4. Check the essentials — empathy shown? measures + justification? consistency? one spokesperson? in line with other actors?
  5. Risk/EPPM — did the message pair the threat with a doable action? panic-limitation vs danger-awareness vs worst-case?
  6. Audience — what was the public asked to do (stay calm, stay home, donate, act)?
  7. Evaluate — did it work, and what would you change? This is where the top marks live.

And remember to name the theorists: Coombs, Benoit, Witte, Slovic, Furedi, Iyengar.
