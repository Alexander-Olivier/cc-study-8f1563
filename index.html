Chapter 1

Foundations — how a crisis works

📘 The vocabulary you'll use everywhere

📘 The theory (read once, then meet it in the stories)

A crisis has a few definitions — learn one and name the author:

  • Coombs (2007): the perception of an unpredictable event that threatens stakeholders' important expectancies and can seriously damage performance. The key word is perception — which is why communication, not just events, decides the outcome.
  • Fearn-Banks (1996): a major occurrence with a negative outcome that interrupts normal business and can threaten the organisation's existence.

Every crisis is unexpected, threatening, demands rapid action, sits where you have limited control, and grabs the media and public. And it has three dimensions: the actual event, how you handle it, and the image left behind.

📌 Your scorecard: the 4 credibility dimensions

The public judges you on openness/honesty · competence · fairness · empathy. In every story below, watch which of the four the people win or lose — that's how you grade a response. BP lost on honesty (it hid the spill's size), competence (it underestimated it) and empathy ("I'd like my life back").

Now the punchline of the whole course, in one line: you can manage the event well and still lose on image — exactly what "winning the war, losing the story" means. Chapters 2–8 are six stories of people who lost (or won) on image. Let's go.

Chapter 2

SCCT — match your response to the blame

🎬 Story: BP & the Deepwater Horizon oil spill

Gulf of Mexico · 20 April 2010 · 11 dead · 4.9 million barrels · the worst spill in US history.

📘 The theory (the quick version)

Situational Crisis Communication Theory (W. Timothy Coombs, 2007), built on Attribution Theory: people instinctively look for someone to blame, so match your response to how much blame you'll get.

  • 3 crisis types (rising blame): Victim (you were hit too) a hacker leaks your data. → Accidental (no intent) a supplier's faulty part causes a recall. → Preventable (you should have stopped it) you ignored safety warnings.
  • 2 intensifiers that raise blame: a history of crises + a bad prior reputation.
  • 3 response postures (rising accommodation): Deny (attack the accuser · denial · scapegoat) → Diminish (excuse · justification) → Rebuild (compensation · apology). Plus bolstering (remind people of past good works, praise them, note you're a victim too).
  • The rule: low blame → deny/diminish is fine; high blame → you must rebuild; and never break consistency.

Keep that in your head. Now watch a company get every single piece of it wrong.

🎬 The story

At 9:45 p.m. on 20 April 2010, a bubble of methane that no one had contained came screaming up the drill pipe of the Deepwater Horizon and exploded. The rig — owned by Transocean, leased by BP, its well cemented by Halliburton — became a fireball 50 miles off Louisiana. The blowout preventer, the giant hydraulic last-resort that was supposed to crush the pipe shut, didn't. Eleven men died. Among them Aaron "Bubba" Burkeen, killed on his wedding anniversary, and Shane Roshto, who was twenty-two. The rig burned for a day and a half and sank, leaving a broken pipe on the seabed vomiting oil.

And then came the detail that made this crisis unique in history: you could watch it. Under pressure from Congress, BP was forced to put a live underwater camera — the "spillcam" — on the gushing pipe. It ran 24/7 on every news site for weeks. Hundreds of millions of people watched oil pour into the ocean in real time.

🧪 Theory, live

First, classify it like Coombs would: a preventable crisis (failures BP could control) → maximum blame; extreme severity; a history of safety incidents and a poor environmental reputation — both intensifiers. The verdict writes itself: this demanded the most accommodative posture, rebuild. Remember that — because BP reached for the opposite.

Enter the CEO, Tony Hayward. Facing a maximum-blame disaster, he played the low-blame hand. He pointed at the contractors — "it wasn't our accident… it was their rig, their equipment, their people" (scapegoating). He shrank the spill:

"The Gulf of Mexico is a very big ocean. The amount of oil and dispersant we are putting into it is tiny in relation to the total water volume."Tony Hayward, mid-May 2010 — and the spillcam was showing the opposite, live, on every screen

He called the likely environmental impact "very, very modest" — then, nine days later, called it an "environmental catastrophe." The whiplash itself became the story.

🧪 Theory, live

Two textbook errors. "Tiny… modest" is minimisation and justification — the diminish posture, fatally wrong for a high-blame crisis. And the flip from "modest" to "catastrophe" breaks Coombs' golden rule of consistency — a shifting story reads as a lie. Worse, the spillcam was a permanent, live contradiction of the message: this is the image dimension destroying the handling, in real time.

Then Hayward stood in front of the cameras on an oiled Louisiana beach — eleven men dead, fishermen ruined — and said the sentence that ended his career:

"There's no one who wants this thing over more than I do. I would like my life back."Tony Hayward, 30 May 2010

The dead men don't get their lives back. He apologised for the line two days later — which only kept it in the news. And on 19 June, while oil still gushed, Hayward took a Saturday off to watch his yacht race off the Isle of Wight, in clean English water. The White House's Rahm Emanuel twisted the knife: "Tony Hayward is not going to have a second career in PR consulting."

🧪 Theory, live

Score it on the 4 credibility dimensions: empathy (gone — he made himself the victim), fairness (gone — he yachted while the Gulf drowned), honesty and competence (gone — the spillcam). A spokesperson can amplify a crisis; Hayward is the case study for why the plan demands a prepared, empathetic one.

Meanwhile BP lost control of its own voice. A parody account, @BPGlobalPR, mocked the company's spin so well it gathered roughly 175,000 followers — about ten times BP's real account. A "Boycott BP" movement spread. President Obama, asked what he was doing, said he was talking to experts "so I know whose ass to kick." Days later, hauled into the Oval Office, BP agreed to a $20 billion victims' fund and suspended its dividend. BP also poured ~$50 million into ads where Hayward promised "We will make this right" — and the spending itself became a fresh grievance.

🧪 Theory, live

The $20bn fund is rebuild (compensation) — but forced out months late, by a president, after the deny/diminish phase had already wrecked BP's image. Right strategy, catastrophic timing. And @BPGlobalPR is the lesson that you no longer own your narrative: the public, and a parody account, will write it for you if you don't.

Hayward was pushed out in October, replaced by the American Bob Dudley. In 2012 BP pleaded guilty to 11 felony manslaughter counts — one for each dead man. The total cost reached about $65 billion.

💡 The lesson (and the mirror image)

A maximum-blame crisis answered with deny + diminish, through a self-pitying spokesperson, with a live camera proving every minimisation false. When you own the crisis, denial doesn't reduce blame — it adds arrogance and dishonesty on top. The fix was always rebuild: accept it, fix it, compensate, and put one empathetic voice in front.

To prove you understand matching, contrast BP's 2025 shareholder revolt — an accidental/management crisis (a strategy U-turn, no misconduct), where the correct match was the opposite: diminish + corrective action, no apology ("we went too far, too fast"; a leadership change). Same company, different blame, opposite right answer. That contrast is SCCT.

❓ Exam questions

Q. Explain SCCT and how a manager selects a response, using BP.

Model answer

SCCT (Coombs) says to match the response to attributed blame (Attribution Theory). Assess the threat via type, severity, history, reputation; classify as victim / accidental / preventable; then choose deny → diminish → rebuild accordingly, and stay consistent. BP shows the failure of a mismatch: a preventable, high-severity crisis with intensifiers (safety history, weak reputation) demanded rebuild, but Hayward used scapegoating ("their rig, their people") and minimisation ("the Gulf is a very big ocean") — then broke consistency ("very modest" → "catastrophe"). The live spillcam exposed the gap, the brand was hijacked by @BPGlobalPR, and a $20bn rebuild came too late. Verdict: under-accommodation in a high-blame crisis reads as arrogance and dishonesty.

Q. Why was Tony Hayward such a damaging spokesperson? Tie it to theory.

Model answer

He failed every credibility dimension: empathy ("I'd like my life back" centred himself over 11 dead), fairness (yacht racing mid-spill), honesty/competence (minimising what the spillcam showed). In SCCT terms he chose diminish for a rebuild situation and broke consistency. The plan's rule is a single, prepared, empathetic spokesperson; Hayward is the cautionary opposite — a spokesperson who amplified the crisis instead of containing it.

Q. A factory explosion is caused by a valve the firm knew was faulty but didn't replace. Classify and prescribe.

Model answer

Preventable, high blame (the prior warning is an aggravator). SCCT prescribes rebuild: lead with instructing/adjusting information, then apology/mortification, compensation and corrective action — never the deny/diminish that sank BP. One consistent, empathetic spokesperson; support with light bolstering (safety record) without it sounding like an excuse.

Chapter 3

Image Repair — defending a reputation

🎬 Story: Carrefour, China & the wheelchair torchbearer

China · 2008, Beijing Olympics year · a French shop boycotted for something it never did.

📘 The theory (the quick version)

Where SCCT asks "how much are you to blame?", Image Repair Theory asks "what's the accusation, and who's the audience?" It grows from Apologia — the rhetoric of self-defence (not "apology"): denial, bolstering, differentiation, transcendence. Benoit (1995) turned it into five categories:

  1. Denial — simple denial / shift the blame. "we didn't do it / our supplier did."
  2. Evasion of responsibility — provocation, defeasibility (no control), accident, good intentions. "a power failure we couldn't control."
  3. Reducing offensiveness — bolstering, minimisation, differentiation, transcendence, attack the accuser, compensation. "only a few affected, and we've been trusted for decades."
  4. Corrective action — fix it / prevent recurrence.
  5. Mortification — admit it and apologise.

One thing to flag in any essay: IRT is descriptive, not evaluative (it lists moves, doesn't prove which works), and it can be misused to help the guilty. Now the story — which begins not with a shop, but with a girl in a wheelchair.

🎬 The story

7 April 2008, Paris. China's Olympic torch is winding from the Eiffel Tower, and the whole world is watching — because 2008 is China's coming-out party. Pro-Tibet protesters keep lunging at the flame; security extinguishes it about five times and bundles it onto a bus. Then comes the image that detonates Chinese national feeling: Jin Jing, a 27-year-old Paralympic fencer whose leg was amputated at eight, is carrying the torch from her wheelchair when a protester grabs for it. She curls her body over the flame and refuses to let go. She later says she "would die to protect the torch." Overnight she is a national hero — "the Angel in a Wheelchair."

China is now a raw nerve of wounded pride. And you cannot march on "France." But you can march on the French shop down the road — and there were more than a hundred of them: Carrefour. A rumour ignites online: that Carrefour, or a big shareholder, was funding the Dalai Lama. It was false — a garbled chain of association — but it didn't matter. Nationalist "angry youth" (fenqing) spread the call by forum, QQ and SMS: "Boycott French goods — start with Carrefour." Around 2.3 million people stuck a ❤ and "China" in their messenger names. Crowds with placards massed outside Carrefour stores on the May Day holiday; some burned flags.

🧪 Theory, live

Here is Benoit's opening question made flesh. There was an accusation ("you fund Tibetan separatism") with no offensive act behind it — Carrefour was a pure victim of "guilt by association." So the whole job is image repair aimed at one audience: the furious Chinese public. Kill the accusation, and the threat dies.

And here Carrefour made its one big mistake: it said nothing for about a week. In a firestorm spreading by SMS and forum, silence was read as indifference, even arrogance — and it hardened the boycott. Only on 22–23 April did Carrefour respond, and when it did, it did it by the textbook. It denied the false charge flatly ("groundless… no direct or indirect support to any political or religious group"). It wrapped itself in Chinese pride — "Carrefour will go all out to support the Beijing Olympics" — and validated the public's anger instead of arguing with it:

"The recent sabotage incidents in Paris during the torch relay hurt the feelings of the Chinese people, made them angry and triggered their protests."José Luis Durán, Carrefour CEO — empathy + ingratiation, not a counter-attack

It leaned hard on a single localising fact: Carrefour employed roughly 40,000 Chinese people. Not "we're French," but "we're basically yours."

🧪 Theory, live

A clean IRT recipe: denial (category 1) of the false charge + reducing offensiveness (category 3) through ingratiation (praising the Olympics, the "40,000 Chinese employees"). No corrective action or mortification — because Carrefour was innocent. Crucially, it refused to argue with the emotion and instead aligned with what the audience valued. That's answering Benoit's "who is the audience?" correctly.

Then two more actors. First, the Chinese government, which had quietly enjoyed the pressure on France — but as the May Day protests threatened to make China look chaotic right before its Olympics, it cooled things down: it told citizens to be "rationally patriotic," publicly praised Carrefour for employing 40,000 Chinese and supporting the Games, briefly censored searches for "家乐福" (Carrefour), and arrested 11 picketers. The state, not the company, ended the crisis.

And the twist that tells you everything about mobs: Jin Jing herself — the Angel in a Wheelchair — spoke out against the boycott, warning it would hurt Carrefour's Chinese staff. The same internet that had crowned her now branded her a traitor, "paid off by the French." The hero became the enemy in a fortnight.

💡 The lesson

When you're innocent but accused, the danger is the accusation, not an act — so move fast (silence let a rumour become "truth"), deny only the factual charge, and align with the audience's emotion rather than fighting it. It is the clean, low-blame counterpart to a guilty case (compare Qatargate, Chapter 8). And note the bigger truth: this crisis was narrative-driven, not fact-driven — born of a false story riding wounded pride, and ended when the state decided the story had served its purpose.

❓ Exam questions

Q. Analyse Carrefour's 2008 crisis using Image Repair Theory.

Model answer

Carrefour was a victim of guilt by association: a false rumour (an accusation with no real offensive act), so the task was image repair before the Chinese public. Its error was a week of silence that, in a viral SMS/forum firestorm, read as arrogance and hardened the boycott. Its recovery combined denial (category 1 — "groundless… no support to any political group") with reducing offensiveness (category 3) via ingratiation (backing the Olympics; stressing its 40,000 Chinese employees) and visible empathy ("the incidents in Paris hurt the feelings of the Chinese people"). It refused to argue with the emotion and aligned with the audience's values — answering Benoit's "who is the audience?" correctly. The case also shows the limits of corporate PR: the state, by calling for "rational patriotism," actually ended it.

Q. Compare apologia and Benoit's IRT; note strengths and limits.

Model answer

Apologia is the older rhetoric of self-defence (denial, bolstering, differentiation, transcendence) plus corporate routes (conciliation, redefinition, dissociation). Benoit systematised and expanded it into five categories with sub-strategies, adding corrective action and mortification and foregrounding the audience. Strengths: comprehensive, adaptable, good for training spokespeople. Limits (shared): descriptive, not evaluative — they don't prove what works — and IRT can be used to help the guilty evade accountability. Carrefour (innocent) and Kaili/Qatargate (accused) show the two ends of its range.

Q. Why is silence dangerous in a social-media crisis?

Model answer

Because silence is itself read as a message — usually as indifference, arrogance or guilt — and in fast peer-to-peer media (forums, SMS, social) the accusation hardens into accepted "truth" while you say nothing. Carrefour's week of silence intensified the boycott before it had said a word; BP's evasions let @BPGlobalPR define its brand. The plan's rule follows: never "no comment" — issue a holding statement fast and keep updating.

Chapter 4

The Crisis Plan — the prevention

📘 The checklist the disasters keep proving

📘 The theory

A plan manages communication through one formal channel to protect a reputation of leadership and transparency. The rules examiners reward:

  • Report to the CEO immediately; only the designated spokesperson speaks.
  • One lead (coordinates) + one spokesperson (faces media). Colectiv failed exactly here — its officials contradicted each other.
  • "No comment" is never acceptable — give a holding statement and a time for the next update.
  • Be proactive, transparent, action-oriented; keep personnel matters confidential; evaluate afterward.

People ask predictable first questions — public: "Am I safe? What can I do? Who caused this? Can you fix it?"; media: "What happened? Who's in charge? Why? Did you have forewarning?" A good first statement answers them. The named decision tool is Kepner-Tregoe (situation appraisal → decision analysis → problem analysis → potential-problem analysis).

🎬 The story — what no plan looks like

You've already seen the plan's value in the negative. BP "had not prepared a crisis communication plan" and improvised — blaming contractors, breaking even its own filed spill plan. Colectiv (Chapter 7) shows the single-voice rule shattering: the emergency chief and the health ministry gave contradictory statements, so neither was believed. The course's own classroom drill makes it concrete: you're a school principal, and an anonymous email says a gunman is coming today — write the statement to media and the email to families. The plan answers it: confirm safety first, name one spokesperson, state the facts and the measures, never say "no comment," promise the next update.

💡 The lesson

Preparation isn't bureaucracy — it's the difference between one calm voice and a public food-fight between officials. Every failure in this guide would have been smaller with the plan's two rules: one voice and never "no comment."

❓ Exam questions

Q. Why is "no comment" never acceptable, and what should you say instead?

Model answer

"No comment" reads as evasion or guilt and hands the narrative to others, failing the credibility dimensions of openness and competence. Instead give a holding statement: confirm what's verified, express concern, describe the immediate measures, and commit to when the next update comes — answering the public's and media's first questions while protecting accuracy and keeping you as the authoritative source.

Q. Outline a first response to a chemical leak near homes.

Model answer

Checklist: safety (secure site, support evacuation) → notify CEO + convene team (Safety, Legal, Operations, Gov Relations) → before going public (one lead + one spokesperson, establish facts) → going public (internal + authorities first, then a media conference). Content follows the first-message logic: concern for residents → what happened + measures (evacuation zone, air monitoring) → what people should do → who's in charge → next update time, pairing the threat with a doable action (EPPM). Never "no comment"; one consistent voice with the local authorities; evaluate continuously.

Chapter 5

The first message to a frightened public

🎬 Story: four European terror attacks

Nice 2016 (86†) · Brussels 2016 (32†) · London Bridge 2017 (7†) · Barcelona 2017 (13†).

📘 The theory

A terror attack inflicts death and deliberately manufactures panic, fear and division — in a "risk society" already primed for fear (Furedi). The first official message must reach three audiences: media, public, and internal staff. The course finds a five-part template:

📌 The first-message template
  1. Sympathy for victims and families.
  2. Information on events + measures taken.
  3. Appreciation for the emergency services and police.
  4. Unity & triumph over the threat — the "round-the-flag" effect.
  5. Invoke the values the attack targeted — democracy, liberty, human rights.

🎬 The story

Because the nation is a victim, there's no blame to deny — so the job isn't defence, it's meaning and morale. Four leaders, within hours, reached for the same five beats — and the differences are where their politics show.

Charles Michel, after the Brussels bombings, chose restraint: "What we feared has happened… I call for calm and solidarity. We have to all face this unified as one." Theresa May, after London Bridge, turned to the police and the flag — "the police responded with great courage and great speed… I want to thank and pay tribute to their professionalism and bravery" — then added a four-point counter-terror plan and the campaign line "enough is enough." François Hollande, after the Nice truck attack, was the most emotional ("horror," "massacre") and turned outward to military strikes in Syria and Iraq, insisting France "is strong and it will always be stronger." Mariano Rajoy, after Barcelona, put victims first and framed terror globally — "Madrid, Paris, Nice, Brussels, Berlin and London have all felt the same pain."

🧪 Theory, live

All four hit the same template; they differ in emotion (Hollande highest, Michel lowest), the call to action (May: domestic security; Hollande: foreign military), and how they read national history. The point of the message is to deny the attackers their goal — fear and division — by replacing panic with solidarity (the "round-the-flag" effect).

💡 The lesson

In a blameless crisis, lead with the people, not the policy: sympathy → facts → thanks → unity → values. This is the very script your US–Iran presentation argued the US skipped when it opened with victory and threat instead of consolation.

❓ Exam questions

Q. What should a first message after a terror attack contain? Support with the cases.

Model answer

Three audiences (media, public, internal staff) and five elements: sympathy → information + measures → appreciation → unity/triumph → invoke values. All four cases share the skeleton — Michel ("calm and solidarity… unified as one"), May (thanking the police), Hollande (France's strength), Rajoy (global solidarity) — and differ in emotion and the call to action. The aim is to deny the attackers their goal (fear, division) by replacing panic with solidarity.

Q. Compare two leaders' responses.

Model answer

May (London) vs Hollande (Nice): same template, different emphasis. May was political and security-focused — police praise, a four-point plan, "enough is enough" during an election. Hollande was more emotive ("horror," "massacre") and turned to military action abroad. The same script carried different politics: domestic reform versus an emotional, external response.

Chapter 6

Risk, fear & EPPM

🎬 Story: COVID-19 across the UK, France & Romania

📘 The theory

Communicating risk is not handing people data. Risk perception (Slovic) explains why we over-fear the spectacular and under-fear the common: "people respond to the hazards they perceive." Perceived risk rises with two factor-families — dread (uncontrollable, catastrophic, involuntary) and the unknown (new, delayed, invisible). Furedi calls the result a "culture of fear." a nuclear meltdown feels far scarier than a car, which kills more people.

🧪 EPPM (Kim Witte) — the rule that matters

A fear message works only if high threat is paired with high efficacy. Show the danger is real — perceived susceptibility + severity — and give a doable action that works — response efficacy + self-efficacy. Then people do danger control (act). Threat with no workable action → fear control (denial, avoidance). "this virus can infect anyone (susceptibility) and can be serious (severity) — but a mask and 20 seconds of handwashing cut your risk (efficacy) and are easy today."

🎬 The story

COVID was a crisis made entirely of communication: the whole task was persuading millions to change their behaviour. Three governments, three styles.

The UK was a tale of two voices. Boris Johnson was informal — telling people to sing "Happy Birthday" twice while washing hands — non-apologetic, and overconfident, leaning on justification, transcendence and a "British exceptionalism" differentiation. Then, on 5 April 2020, the Queen spoke — and gave the course's model address: calm, rational, anchored in unity and "national spirit," appealing to pride, and pointedly never saying the words "pandemic," "coronavirus" or "crisis." She made no false promises. Two registers from one country: threat-management versus reassurance.

France made accessibility its strategy — a chatbot, the TousAntiCovid app, easy-read documents with pictograms, material in 24 languages and sign-language briefings. PM Castex was clear but stumbled (blamed citizens; admitted not downloading the app); his speeches are a clinic in the toolkit — transcendence ("all of Europe faces waves"), differentiation (France's results "more than honourable"), ingratiation (thanking citizens) and EPPM efficacy. Macron went full warrior rhetoric — "a first victory against the virus." Romania showed how delivery sinks content: decrees dumped at 11 p.m., jargon, a PM who rushed and mispronounced, and a government forced to fight a top-ten list of vaccine myths with warnings rather than thanks.

🧪 Theory, live

COVID is the "everything at once" case — Coombs (justification, ingratiation), Benoit (transcendence, differentiation), and above all EPPM. The pattern: leaders won by pairing realistic threat with a clear, doable action (and, like the Queen, calm + shared identity); they lost when they scared without efficacy, blamed citizens, or delivered badly.

❓ Exam questions

Q. Explain EPPM and use it to fix a failing message.

Model answer

Witte's EPPM: a fear message works only if threat (susceptibility + severity) is matched with efficacy (response + self) → danger control; without efficacy, people default to fear control (denial, avoidance). Failing message: "A deadly virus is spreading and there's nothing you can do" → panic/fatalism. Fixed: "This virus spreads fast and can be serious (susceptibility + severity); handwashing and a mask significantly cut your risk and you can do them today (response + self-efficacy)."

Q. Why do people fear terrorism more than far deadlier everyday risks?

Model answer

Because perception ≠ statistics (Slovic). Terrorism scores high on dread (uncontrollable, catastrophic, involuntary) and the unknown (unpredictable), so it feels riskier than driving, which is familiar and controllable. Furedi's "culture of fear" and social amplification spread the signal. Implication: address the perception and pair any threat with a workable action (EPPM).

Q. Compare the Queen and Boris Johnson on COVID.

Model answer

The Queen's 5 April 2020 address is the model: calm, rational, unity and national spirit, appealing to pride, deliberately avoiding "pandemic/crisis," no false promises — high on empathy and competence. Johnson was informal and non-apologetic, overconfident, using justification, transcendence, differentiation ("British exceptionalism") and EPPM threat — strong on conveying danger, weaker on reassurance and consistency. Two registers: shared-identity reassurance versus threat-management.

Chapter 7

Framing & the public sector

🎬 Story: the Colectiv fire

Bucharest · 30 October 2015 · 27 dead that night → 64 · it brought down a government.

📘 The theory

Two ideas the exam likes. Public-sector communication: institutions must take social media seriously (not one-way posting), keep one consistent voice, and remember a crisis has two facets — the event and its perception. And framing (Shanto Iyengar): how the media tell the story decides who gets blamed. Episodic framing (one event/victim) → blame the individual. a report following one grieving family → "the negligent club owner." Thematic framing (context/system) → blame the system. a report on bribed safety inspections → "systemic corruption."

🎬 The story

30 October 2015. The metal band Goodbye to Gravity is playing a free album launch in Club Colectiv, a converted Bucharest factory licensed for 80 people and holding maybe 400. The walls are lined with cheap, non-fireproof foam. The band sets off indoor sparkler "fountains." A spark hits a foam pillar. The singer, Andrei Găluț, sees the flame and jokes into the mic — "this wasn't part of the program" — then realises in a heartbeat it's real and shouts for a fire extinguisher.

There isn't time. In about 30 seconds the fire races across the ceiling and the foam pours out black, toxic smoke. There is one exit, 80 centimetres wide. Hundreds funnel toward it and crush together. Twenty-seven die that night; most of the band is killed. Găluț is the only band member to survive.

But here is what turned a tragedy into a national reckoning: the death toll kept climbing for weeks — doubling to 64 — as burn victims died not of their burns but of drug-resistant infections in Romanian hospitals. There were reports of maggots in the wounds of living patients. And officials — including the health minister and the emergency chief Raed Arafat — kept insisting Romanian hospitals were as good as Germany's, so patients were not sent abroad in time. Parents later said their children died because transfer was refused.

🧪 Theory, live

This is the gap between message and reality — reassurance over truth — at its most lethal. The false "we're as good as Germany" failed the credibility dimension of honesty, and unlike a normal PR failure it cost lives, because the message justified a deadly decision. Note too the SCCT failure: officials' statements contradicted each other, and the state used Facebook only to post, never to interact (one official post: 99 shares, a single reply).

Then the second scandal broke — and the hero was the press. A sports newspaper, Gazeta Sporturilor, led by editor Cătălin Tolontan, revealed that the country's main hospital disinfectant supplier, Hexi Pharma, had been selling products diluted up to ten times — effectively soapy water — useless against the superbugs killing the burn victims, while paying kickbacks to hospitals. The owner, Dan Condrea, died in a violent car crash the day before he was due to testify. The exposé was later filmed in the Oscar-nominated documentary Collective.

"When the press bows down to the authorities, the authorities will mistreat the citizens."Cătălin Tolontan, in the documentary Collective
🧪 Theory, live

Now framing decides the politics. The protest movement seized a thematic frame — "Corupția ucide" ("Corruption Kills") — that pinned the deaths not on one club owner but on a system of bribed permits and graft. That systemic frame is exactly why the consequence was systemic: the largest protests since 1989, and Prime Minister Victor Ponta's government resigned within nine days. An episodic frame ("a careless club owner") would have contained the blame; the thematic frame brought down a government.

💡 The lesson

Two things stack. First, being factually right isn't enough — and a reassuring message that's false can kill, not just mislead. Second, whoever frames the story shapes the blame: episodic vs thematic framing is how a single fire became a national reckoning. Colectiv is your best "use four tools at once" case — Coombs + Benoit + the plan (one voice) + Iyengar.

❓ Exam questions

Q. Define episodic vs thematic framing (Iyengar) and apply to Colectiv.

Model answer

Episodic framing tells an issue through specific events/individuals → audiences blame the individual; thematic framing sets it in context/trends → audiences blame the system. At Colectiv, an episodic frame ("one negligent club owner") would have contained blame; instead the movement framed it thematically as "Corruption Kills" — systemic graft and bribed safety permits. That thematic frame mobilised the biggest protests since 1989 and forced the resignation of the Prime Minister and government. Framing was the decisive force directing public blame.

Q. Why did the public authorities' communication fail at Colectiv?

Model answer

Several reasons: responsibility was diffuse and political (owners, inspectors, ministries); officials gave contradictory statements, breaking the single-voice rule; the lead voice (Arafat) was competent but emotionally distant and defensive; the state used social media only to post, not interact; and, most damningly, the reassurance that Romanian hospitals matched Germany's was false and contributed to deaths by delaying transfers. The course's lesson: the public sector should adopt private-sector communication discipline and treat the perception facet of a crisis as seriously as the event.

Chapter 8

Political crisis & tailored image repair

🎬 Story: Qatargate & Eva Kaili

European Parliament · raids 9 Dec 2022 · suitcases of cash · a defence in three languages.

⚖️ A note on fairness

Everything about Kaili here is alleged; she maintains her innocence, and as of writing there has been no trial or conviction. We're studying the communication, not pronouncing guilt.

📘 The theory

This pushes Image Repair Theory to its hardest test and answers Benoit's second question — who is the audience? The strategies are the familiar IRT menu (denial, attack-the-accuser, transcendence, victimage), but the lesson is tailoring and reframing: the same accusation answered with a different mix for different publics, and offering an alternative frame when the media have already written your story.

🎬 The story

9 December 2022. Belgian police fan out across Brussels in a single morning of raids — and start finding cash. In suitcases. About €1.5 million in all: €600,000 at the home of a former MEP, more at other flats. The most cinematic detail: Eva Kaili's own father is caught trying to leave a Brussels hotel with a suitcase of cash.

Kaili is not a minor figure. She's a Vice-President of the European Parliament, a glamorous former TV news presenter from Greece. Because her father is caught in the act, her parliamentary immunity evaporates and she's arrested on the spot. The alleged scheme: a former MEP, Panzeri, running an NGO called — without irony — "Fight Impunity," channelling money from Qatar (eager to soften criticism before its World Cup) and Morocco to buy influence inside the Parliament. Within days Kaili is stripped of her vice-presidency, expelled from her party, and sent to Haren prison. Her partner confesses — and says she wasn't involved.

🧪 Theory, live

By SCCT this is a preventable-type crisis (alleged misconduct) with maximum blame and a hostile media that already had its villain. With guilt presumed, simple denial wasn't going to work — Kaili had to reframe the entire story. Watch her do exactly what Benoit's "who is the audience?" implies.

From April 2023, out of prison and on to house arrest, Kaili launched a communication counter-offensive — and the masterstroke was that she told a different story to each country:

On the cash, the course notes her line that gifts were "souvenirs we exchanged for gifts of equal value" (she offered to show a perfume, a statuette, promotional glasses) — recasting "bribe" as "ordinary diplomacy." She even rebranded the scandal's name: not "Qatargate" but "Belgiangate" — turning the wrongdoing back onto her prosecutors. It helped her that the lead judge later had to recuse himself for a conflict of interest, a gift to the "the investigation is the real scandal" line. And unlike the others, she refused a plea deal — which let her hold a single, consistent posture: I am innocent and I am fighting.

🧪 Theory, live

One defendant, three audiences, three emotional registers: rights-abuse for France, spy-conspiracy for Spain, maternal sympathy for Italy. That's audience-tailored image repair — Benoit's "who is the audience?" taken to its extreme — plus reframing ("Qatargate" → "Belgiangate") and attacking the accuser (the prosecutors). The strategies are the same Carrefour used; the artistry is the tailoring.

💡 The lesson

The same facts can support several frames; pick the one that fits the audience you most need to win. Qatargate is the advanced counterpart to Carrefour: Carrefour was innocent and needed to kill a rumour fast; Kaili was accused and needed to reframe a story the media had already set. Together they show IRT's full range — and its ethical edge: a persuasive defence is not proof of innocence.

❓ Exam questions

Q. How did Eva Kaili tailor her image repair to different audiences, and why does it matter for IRT?

Model answer

Facing one accusation, she ran three interviews with three frames: judicial injustice in France (Libération), a Pegasus spying conspiracy in Spain (El Mundo), and motherhood in Italy (Corriere), mixing denial, attack-the-accuser, transcendence and victimage to suit each public, and rebranding "Qatargate" as "Belgiangate." This dramatises Benoit's second question — who is the audience? — showing strategy choice is audience-dependent: the right mix is the one a given public will validate. It's also a case of reframing to seize a narrative the media had already written.

Q. Contrast Carrefour and Kaili as image-repair cases.

Model answer

Carrefour was a victim of a false rumour (accusation, no act); it needed speed, simple denial + ingratiation, and alignment with the audience's values — its lesson is the cost of silence. Kaili was an accused figure with a hostile, pre-written narrative; she couldn't just deny, so she reframed and tailored her defence to three national audiences — her lesson is the audience-dependence and narrative control of IRT. Together they map the theory's full range: defending innocence vs repairing a damaged, contested reputation.

🧩 How to write a case answer

Whatever case you're given, walk this path — it mirrors the analysis you just did six times:

  1. Describe the crisis — events, actors, who's responsible, the media response.
  2. Classify the type (victim / accident / preventable) — did it shift? add intensifiers (history, reputation).
  3. Name the strategy — SCCT posture and/or Benoit's categories, with exact sub-strategies and quotes.
  4. Check the essentials — empathy? measures? consistency? one spokesperson? aligned with other actors?
  5. Risk/EPPM — did the message pair the threat with a doable action?
  6. Audience — what was the public asked to do?
  7. Evaluate — did it work, and what would you change? This is where the marks live.

And name the theorists: Coombs, Benoit, Witte, Slovic, Furedi, Iyengar.
