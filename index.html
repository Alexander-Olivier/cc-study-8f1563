🌊1. What is a crisis?

Three definitions from the course. Learn one well and be able to name the author.

Coombs (2007)

The perception of an unpredictable event that threatens stakeholders' important expectancies and can seriously damage performance.

Fearn-Banks (1996)

A major occurrence with a potentially negative outcome; it interrupts normal business and can threaten the organisation's existence.

Seeger, Sellnow & Ulmer (1998): a specific, unexpected, non-routine event creating high uncertainty and threat to an organisation's high-priority goals.

The anatomy of a crisis 🧬

Unexpected · poses a threat · potentially hazardous · needs rapid action · management has limited control · unpredictable effects · grabs media + public attention.

📌 Key: the 4 credibility dimensions

The public rates an authority on: openness/honesty · competence · fairness · empathy. Use these four as a quick scorecard in any case analysis.

🧠 Memory hook

Crisis = the 3 dimensions: the actual crisis, how you handle it, and the image. You can lose on image even if you handled the event — exactly the point of "winning the war, losing the story." 😉

⚖️2. SCCT — Situational Crisis Communication Theory ⭐

W. Timothy Coombs (2007). The headline idea: match your response to how much the public blames you (the attributed responsibility) and to the reputational threat. It's built on Attribution Theory — people look for someone to blame; the more blame, the more you must accommodate.

Step 1 — Size up the reputational threat (4 factors)

  • Crisis type (sets the baseline responsibility)
  • Severity of the harm
  • History of similar crises
  • Prior reputation / relationship
⚠️ The 2 "intensifying factors"

A history of crises and a prior negative reputation push any crisis toward higher blame. Same event, worse position if you've got baggage.

Step 2 — The 3 crisis types (low → high blame)

ClusterBlameCourse examples👉 Everyday example
VictimMinimalnatural disaster, rumour, product tampering, workplace violenceAn earthquake damages your factory; a hacker leaks your customer data.
AccidentalLowtechnical-error accident, challengesA faulty part from a supplier causes a recall — you didn't intend it.
PreventableStronghuman error, organisational misdeed, management misconductYour CEO is caught lying to regulators; you ignored safety warnings.

Step 3 — The response strategies

🚫 DENY (reject blame)

  • Attack the accuser
  • Denial — there is no crisis
  • Scapegoat — blame someone outside

🪶 DIMINISH (shrink blame)

  • Excuse — we didn't intend harm
  • Justification — the damage is small

🛠️ REBUILD (most accommodative)

  • Compensation — money/gifts to victims
  • Apology — full responsibility + ask forgiveness

➕ BOLSTERING (secondary support)

  • Reminder — our past good works
  • Ingratiation — praise stakeholders
  • Victimage — we're victims too
📌 The matching rule (+ the golden rule)

Low blame (victim) → informing/adjusting info, deny/diminish. High blame (preventable) or any intensifying factor → rebuild (apologise, compensate, fix). And above all: maintain consistency. A shifting story reads as deception — the single most testable SCCT principle.

📚 Case: BP — Deepwater Horizon (2010)

The rig (Transocean-owned, BP-leased) exploded: 11 dead, 4.9m barrels spilled. Type = preventable/accident, high responsibility.

  • ❌ Started with deny + shift blame ("it was Transocean's rig") and minimisation ("environmental impact… very modest") — wrong posture for high blame.
  • ❌ Spokesperson Tony Hayward was arrogant & self-focused: "I'd like my life back." No crisis plan. Tried to limit the media. Offered plaintiffs $5,000 to drop suits.
  • ✅ Later rebuild: $20bn fund, "We will make this right" ads, research initiative.
  • 📉 Result: lost ~25% of market value. Textbook mismatch + broken consistency.
🎯 Exam move

If you classify a crisis as preventable but the org responds with deny/diminish, that mismatch is your analysis. Say it explicitly and name the fix (rebuild + consistent, empathetic single voice).

🗣️3. Apologia & Corporate Apologia

Apologia = the rhetoric of self-defence (Greek apologia) — not "an apology". An organisation may deny, explain, or apologise through rhetorical strategies.

The 4 classic strategies

  • Denial — I wasn't involved.
  • Bolstering — look at all my good deeds.
  • Differentiation — take the act out of its bad context (compare to something worse).
  • Transcendence — put the act in a bigger, more favourable context.

Corporate apologia — 3 strategies

🤝 Conciliation

Admit guilt, apologise, offer compensation + corrective action.

🎭 Redefinition

Diffuse the public's perception of guilt — via provocation (we reacted to hostility), defeasibility (forces beyond control), good intentions, minimisation.

🔌 Dissociation

Separate the org from the crisis — opinion–knowledge (facts say otherwise), individual–group (one person, not us all), accident–essence (isolated, not who we are).

⚠️ The catch (Pros/Cons)

Apologia is a brilliant foundation and named many response types first — but it's descriptive, not evaluative: it doesn't tell you which strategy actually works. (Same limitation as IRT.)

🩹4. Image Repair Theory — Benoit (1995) ⭐

A classification of strategies to restore a damaged image after an attack. An attack = an offensive act + an accusation of responsibility (remove either and there's no threat). Benoit says first ask: What is the accusation? and Who is the key audience? (audiences validate or reject what you project).

💡 One running example: the "BrewCo" milk scare ☕

Invented to make the 5 categories stick. Imagine your favourite coffee chain, BrewCo, is accused of serving contaminated milk that made customers sick. Here's how each repair strategy sounds:

Benoit's 5 categories — with the BrewCo line for each
CategorySub-strategies☕ BrewCo says…
1. Denialsimple denial · shifting the blame"Our milk was fine — it was the supplier's fault."
2. Evasion of responsibilityprovocation · defeasibility · accident · good intentions"The cold-chain failed in transit — beyond our control."
3. Reducing offensivenessbolstering · minimisation · differentiation · transcendence · attack accuser · compensation"Only a few cups affected, and we've served safe coffee for 30 years."
4. Corrective actionfix it / prevent recurrence"We've recalled the batch and added daily safety tests."
5. Mortificationadmit + apologise"We are truly sorry. We take full responsibility."
🧠 Memory hook

D-E-R-C-M → "Don't Even Repair Crises Messily." Denial, Evasion, Reducing offensiveness, Corrective action, Mortification.

📚 Case: Carrefour boycott, China (2008)

During the Beijing Olympics, rumours spread that Carrefour funded the Dalai Lama → online + physical boycott. A classic "guilt by association" victim situation.

  • ❌ Stayed silent for a week (a mistake).
  • ✅ Then: denial of pro-Tibet links + adjusting information + sympathy + ingratiation (praised the Olympics & China).
  • ✅ Rumour-debunk in 3 steps: (1) acknowledge why people might believe it, (2) explain why it's untrue, (3) state the truth (neutral on politics/religion).

VP Lhuillier: "Carrefour is innocent, but we understand the feeling of the Chinese people." The government, conflicted, urged "rational" patriotism.

🎲5. Risk Perception & EPPM (Witte)

Big idea: communicating risk is not just handing people data. Risk perception explains why people fear what they fear — and they over-fear spectacular, uncertain hazards while under-fearing common ones. "People respond to the hazards they perceive" (Slovic, Fischhoff & Lichtenstein 1979).

💡 Example

People fear plane crashes far more than car crashes, though driving is statistically far deadlier. Planes feel uncontrollable, catastrophic, dreaded — the perception, not the statistic, drives the fear.

Why fear gets amplified

  • Furedi — "culture of fear" / "risk society": media pump the worst-case, trust in experts declines, suspicion of hidden interests rises.
  • Slovic's psychometric factors: Dread (uncontrollable, catastrophic, fatal, involuntary…) + Unknown (unobservable, delayed, new, unknown to science). More dread + more unknown = more perceived risk.
  • Cultural Theory (Douglas): attitudes compared on grid (roles/control) × group (bonds).
  • Social Amplification of Risk: institutions & social groups amplify or dampen risk signals as they pass through society.

⭐ EPPM — Extended Parallel Process Model (Kim Witte)

A model for effective public-health / crisis messages. It runs on four perceptions:

The threat

  • Perceived susceptibility — could this hit me?
  • Perceived severity — how bad is it?

The efficacy

  • Response efficacy — does the action work?
  • Self-efficacy — can I do it?
📌 The one rule to remember

High threat must be paired with high efficacy. Then people do danger control (act on the danger). Threat with no doable action → fear control (denial, avoidance, tuning out).

💡 Example (done right vs wrong)

✅ "COVID is spreading fast (threat). Wash your hands for 20s and wear a mask — it works (efficacy)." → people act.
❌ "COVID is everywhere and unstoppable." → no action offered → people panic or shut down.

📋6. The Crisis Communication Plan

Purpose: manage communication through a formal, single channel to mitigate the crisis and protect a reputation of leadership and transparency.

⚠️ Rules examiners love
  • "No comment" is NEVER acceptable.
  • Only the designated spokesperson (and back-ups) talk to media.
  • One lead (coordinates) + one spokesperson (faces media).
  • Report immediately · be proactive, transparent, action-oriented · keep personnel matters confidential.

The questions people ask first 🙋

The public asks

Am I/my family safe? What affects me? What can I do? Who caused this? Can you fix it?

The media asks

What happened? Who's in charge? Is it contained? Are victims helped? Why did this happen? Did you have forewarning?

Decision-making: Kepner-Tregoe

Four lenses: situation appraisal (what's going on?) · decision analysis (best option?) · problem analysis (what went wrong?) · potential-problem analysis (what could go wrong?).

Team: CEO · PR/Comms · senior advisors · Finance · Gov Relations · HR · Investor Relations · Operations · Safety · Legal. Checklist: Safety → Notify → Team → Before Going Public → Going Public → Evaluate.

🕯️7. First messages after a terror attack ⭐

A terror attack causes death and destruction plus wider panic and fear. Recovery needs a planned strategy for three audiences: the media, the public, and internal staff. Four cases: Nice 2016, Brussels 2016, London Bridge 2017, Barcelona 2017.

📌 The first-message template (memorise the 5)
  1. Express sympathy for victims & families.
  2. Give information on the situation + measures taken.
  3. Show appreciation for emergency services & police.
  4. Emphasise unity & triumph over the threat ("round-the-flag effect").
  5. Invoke the values the attack targeted: democracy, liberty, human rights, unity.
🧠 Memory hook

S-I-A-U-V → Sympathy, Info, Appreciation, Unity, Values.

How leaders differed: Hollande (Nice) — emotive words + military response; May (London) — round-the-flag, praises police, a 4-step plan, "enough is enough"; Michel (Brussels) — calm, solidarity, unity; Rajoy (Barcelona) — victims first, global framing. The differences are in emotion level, the call-to-action, and how each reads its country's history.

🎯 Exam tip

This is the same template behind your own presentation's "first message" point. If a case is a victim crisis (attack, disaster), grade it against these 5 elements.

🖼️8. Framing (Iyengar) & Colectiv

Episodic vs thematic framing (Shanto Iyengar):

  • Episodic = single events/people → audiences blame the individual. (e.g. "a club owner's negligence.")
  • Thematic = context/trends → audiences blame the system. (e.g. "Corruption Kills" — Romania's systemic failure.)

📚 Case: Colectiv (2015)

Bucharest club fire, 30 Oct 2015, 27→65 dead. Type = accident/preventable, high severity, no prior history.

  • Club owners: partial shifting of blame (point at inspectors).
  • Sec. of State Raed Arafat: technical explanations, transcendence (Europe-wide burn-care limits), attacks the accusers (TV "experts"), little empathy.
  • ❌ Clashing statements between Arafat and the Ministry of Health — a consistency failure.
  • ❌ Public sector ignored social media (info-only, no interaction).
  • ➡️ Political crisis: PM Ponta, government & mayor resigned; President Iohannis consulted civil society.

🧩10. How to answer a CASE question

Use this exact order in the exam — it mirrors the professor's presentation checklist and earns the marks in sequence.

  1. Describe the crisis — events, actors, who's responsible, the media response.
  2. Classify the type (victim / accident / preventable) — note if it shifted + the intensifying factors (history, reputation).
  3. Name the strategy — SCCT posture (deny/diminish/rebuild) and/or Benoit's categories, with exact sub-strategies + quotes.
  4. Check the first-message elements — empathy? measures? justification? appreciation? consistency? aligned with other actors?
  5. Spokesperson — good choice? one voice or clashing voices?
  6. Risk/EPPM — did it pair threat with a doable action? panic-limitation vs danger-awareness?
  7. Audience — what was the public asked to do (stay home, donate, stay calm)?
  8. Evaluate — did it work, and what would you change? Top marks live here.

📝12. Practice exam

Answer in your head (or on paper) first, then tap to check.

Section A — short answer

A1. Define a crisis (cite one author).

Coombs (2007): the perception of an unpredictable event that threatens stakeholders' important expectancies and can seriously impact performance.

A2. Name Benoit's five categories.

Denial · Evasion of responsibility · Reducing offensiveness · Corrective action · Mortification.

A3. What does EPPM say about fear appeals?

Threat (susceptibility + severity) must be paired with efficacy (response + self). No efficacy → fear control (denial/avoidance) instead of danger control.

A4. Two rules from the crisis-plan policy.

"No comment" is never acceptable; only one designated spokesperson (with one lead) communicates with media.

Section B — essay (outline)

B1. Explain SCCT and how a manager picks a response.

Define SCCT (Coombs) + Attribution Theory → assess threat (type, severity, history, reputation) → classify type → choose posture (deny/diminish for low blame; rebuild for high blame or intensifying factors) → maintain consistency. Illustrate with BP (high blame, but denied → reputational collapse).

B2. Compare Apologia and Image Repair Theory.

Apologia = self-defence rhetoric (denial, bolstering, differentiation, transcendence) → corporate apologia (conciliation/redefinition/dissociation). Benoit's IRT systematised & expanded it into 5 categories, adding corrective action + mortification and foregrounding the audience. Both are descriptive, not evaluative; IRT can be misused to dodge accountability. Illustrate with Carrefour + Colectiv.

B3. What should a first message after a terror attack contain?

3 audiences (media/public/internal). 5 elements: sympathy → info + measures → appreciation → unity/triumph → invoke values. Vary with Brussels/Nice/London/Barcelona (emotion, call-to-action, national history).

Section C — case analysis

C1. Analyse BP / Deepwater Horizon with SCCT.

Type = preventable/accident, high responsibility, high severity. BP began with deny + shift-blame + minimisation (wrong posture); arrogant spokesperson (Hayward); no plan; limited media. Later rebuild ($20bn, "make it right"). Verdict: mismatch + broken consistency → ~25% value lost. Fix: lead with rebuild + empathetic single voice.

C2. Transfer task — a hospital gives ~40 patients expired vaccines; a nurse alleges management knew. You're the comms lead. Your plan?
  • Classify: genuine error = accidental (low blame); but "management knew" pushes it toward preventable (high blame) + a reputational intensifier. State both.
  • SCCT: lead with instructing/adjusting info (never "no comment"); given health severity → rebuild: corrective action (recall, retest, audit) + care/compensation + mortification/apology if responsibility is confirmed.
  • First-message: sympathy → facts + free retesting + hotline → appreciation for responders → commitment to investigate.
  • EPPM: pair the threat with a clear doable action (come in for a check) → danger control, not panic.
  • Spokesperson/consistency: one medical spokesperson, one lead, aligned with health ministry & local authorities (avoid the Colectiv "clashing voices" trap).
  • Handle the allegation transparently — don't attack the nurse (it would wreck honesty/fairness). Evaluate as it unfolds.
✅ Self-check rubric

Did you name the theorist? classify the crisis type + intensifying factors? name specific sub-strategies with examples? check empathy · measures · consistency · spokesperson · audience action? and evaluate (what worked, what you'd change)?
